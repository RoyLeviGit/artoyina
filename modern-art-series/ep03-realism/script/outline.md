# Episode 3 Outline: "The First Artist Who Didn't Give a Damn"
## Movement: Realism + Naturalism | Runtime Target: 35–45 min

---

### 1. COLD OPEN (2–3 min)
**Scene:** Paris, 1850. You walk into the Salon. Every painting on the wall is about gods, heroes, ancient battles — the usual. And then you turn a corner and there's this MASSIVE painting — the size of a wall — and it's... a funeral. In a small town. With ugly people. And a dog. Real people, real faces, nobody idealized, nobody heroic. Just death, in a ditch, in the middle of nowhere.

Everyone in Paris loses their mind. One critic calls it "a glorification of the common." The painter — a loud, arrogant man from the provinces — basically says: yeah, and?

That painter was Gustave Courbet. And that painting changed art forever.

---

### 2. "OKAY, LET ME BACK UP" — The World in 1848 (5–8 min)

**The revolutions:**
- 1848: Europe explodes. Revolutions everywhere. In France, the monarchy falls again.
- The June Days: workers vs. the state. Thousands killed. The Romantic dream of revolution meets brutal reality.
- The lesson: maybe it's time to stop dreaming and start looking.

**Photography:**
- The daguerreotype exists. A machine can now capture reality.
- So what's the point of painting it? This is the existential crisis facing every painter.
- Realism's answer: a painting isn't a photograph. It's an interpretation. It shows you what reality *means*.

**The Salon (still):**
- Still the only game in town. Still controlled by conservatives.
- History painting still at the top. Still life at the bottom.
- But the cracks from Episode 2 are widening.

**Daumier — the cartoonist:**
- Brief introduction. Show *Rue Transnonain*. A family massacred by troops. It's not a cartoon — it's journalism in ink.
- Daumier imprisoned for a caricature of the king. Political art has real consequences.
- He proves Realism is a worldview, not just a painting style.

---

### 3. THE MAIN CHARACTER — Courbet (12–15 min)

**The persona:**
- Provincial. Self-taught (mostly). Studied the Old Masters in the Louvre, rejected the Academy.
- Arrogant from the start. Early self-portraits show a man performing different roles — he was building a brand before branding existed.
- "Show me an angel and I'll paint one."

**The Burial at Ornans (1849–1850):**
- This is the centerpiece. Spend real time here.
- 10 × 22 feet. A funeral in his hometown. Real people posed for it.
- Walk through it: the priest, the gravedigger, the mourners, the dog. No one is beautiful. No one is heroic. The grave is just a hole in the ground.
- The scale is the provocation. This is the size reserved for the death of Caesar, the coronation of Napoleon. And it's a funeral in Ornans.
- The critics' fury. "Ugly." "Vulgar." "A masquerade funeral."
- What Courbet understood: painting ordinary people at this scale is a political act. It says their lives matter as much as kings'.

**The Stone Breakers (1849):**
- Two men breaking rocks. Faces turned away. Anonymous. Interchangeable.
- Proudhon saw socialism. Courbet said he just painted what he saw.
- Destroyed in the bombing of Dresden, 1945. We only have photographs. There's something poignant about that — a painting about the disposability of workers, itself disposed of by war.

**Millet — the quiet one:**
- Brief pivot. Same subjects, different temperament.
- *The Gleaners*: three women gleaning. Monumental. Critics called them "scarecrows of poverty."
- *The Angelus*: prayer in a field. Became one of the most reproduced images in history.
- Millet was the real thing — actually from a peasant family. Courbet was performing the role. Both were sincere. Both were accused of being socialists.

---

### 4. THE PAVILION — Courbet Goes Independent (5–7 min)

**1855: The World's Fair rejection:**
- Submits 14 paintings. 11 accepted, but the two most important rejected.
- His response: builds his own exhibition hall across the street. The Pavilion of Realism.
- This is the first independent exhibition in modern art history. The prototype for everything that follows.

**The Realist Manifesto:**
- "To create living art — this is my goal." Put it on screen.
- He didn't want to imitate the ancients or copy the moderns. He wanted to paint *now*.

**The Painter's Studio (1855):**
- His manifesto in paint. Himself at the center. The world of labor on one side. The world of art on the other. Baudelaire is literally in the painting, reading.
- The arrogance is staggering. It's also kind of earned.

---

### 5. THE FALL — The Commune and Exile (5–7 min)

**The scandalous works (brief):**
- *The Origin of the World* — mention it. You probably can't show it uncensored, but describe it. Courbet painted bodies as they are. No idealization. No apology.

**The Franco-Prussian War and the Commune:**
- France defeated. Napoleon III falls. Paris besieged.
- The Commune: 72 days of radical self-government.
- Courbet elected to the council. Put in charge of protecting art.
- The Vendôme Column: a monument to Napoleon's wars. The Commune votes to demolish it. Courbet supports it.
- The Commune crushed. Bloody Week. Thousands killed.
- Courbet arrested. Imprisoned. Ordered to pay for the column's reconstruction.
- Flees to Switzerland. Drinks. Deteriorates.
- Dies on New Year's Eve, 1877. In exile. The debt unpaid.

**The parallel to David:**
- Both painters got entangled in revolutionary politics. Both ended in exile.
- But David switched allegiances (Revolution → Napoleon). Courbet never did. He stayed a radical to the end.
- "He belonged to no school, to no church, to no institution, to no academy, least of all to any régime except the régime of liberty."

---

### 6. THE LEGACY (3–5 min)

**What Courbet proved:**
- You can paint real life. You can paint ugly truth. You can reject the entire system and build your own.
- Every independent exhibition, every artist-run gallery, every punk who said "I'll do it myself" — Courbet was there first.

**The Balzac connection:**
- Mention *The Unknown Masterpiece*. A painter who chased reality so hard he lost it. Courbet was determined not to make that mistake. (Cézanne, in Episode 5, will identify with that story deeply.)

**Closing hook:**
> "Courbet proved you could paint real life. But a group of young painters were about to ask a question he never thought to ask: what does real life actually *look like*? Not what's in front of you — but how your eyes actually *see* it. The light. The color. The moment. They'd be mocked, rejected, and named after an insult. And they'd change everything."
