# Episode 3: "The First Artist Who Didn't Give a Damn"
## FULL SCRIPT — DRAFT v1
### Movement: Realism + Naturalism | Target Runtime: 35–45 min

---

## ACT 1: COLD OPEN

*[No music. Talking head, direct to camera. Storytelling energy — painting a scene.]*

[TALKING HEAD]

Okay so imagine this. It's 1850. You walk into the Salon in Paris — the biggest art exhibition in the world. Every painting on the wall is what you'd expect. Gods. Heroes. Ancient battles. Beautiful women in mythological settings. The usual.

And then you turn a corner and there's this MASSIVE painting — ten feet tall, twenty-two feet wide, the size of an entire wall — and it's... a funeral. In a small town. In the middle of nowhere.

There's a priest. A gravedigger. Mourners in black. A dog. The grave is just a hole in the ground. And the people — the people are *ugly*. Not ugly in a dramatic, Romantic way. Just... regular ugly. Real faces. Red noses. Bad posture. Nobody is idealized. Nobody is heroic. Nobody is even particularly sad in a photogenic way. It's just... a funeral. In a place called Ornans.

And everyone in Paris loses their mind.

One critic calls it "a glorification of the common." Another says it's "a masquerade funeral six meters long in which there is more to laugh at than to weep over." The art establishment is furious. How dare someone paint *this* — at *that* size? That size is for the death of Caesar. For the coronation of Napoleon. Not for a funeral in a village nobody's heard of.

The painter — a loud, arrogant, self-promoting man from the provinces — hears all of this. And his response, essentially, is: yeah. And?

His name was Gustave Courbet. And that painting — *A Burial at Ornans* — changed art forever.

## ACT 2: THE WORLD IN 1848

*[Transition. Underscore — something with weight, grounded.]*

### The Revolutions

[TALKING HEAD]

To understand Courbet, you need to understand 1848. Because 1848 is the year Europe caught fire.

In February, revolution broke out in France — again. King Louis-Philippe was overthrown. The Second Republic was declared. And the revolutionary fever spread: Vienna, Berlin, Milan, Budapest, Prague. They called it the Springtime of Peoples. For a brief, intoxicating moment, it looked like the old order was finally going to fall.

And then it didn't.

In France, the June Days uprising — workers demanding jobs and rights — was crushed by the government. Thousands killed. Thousands more deported. And within three years, Louis-Napoleon — Napoleon's nephew — staged a coup and declared himself Emperor Napoleon III. The Republic was dead. Again.

The Romantic dream of revolution — the dream Delacroix had painted in *Liberty Leading the People* — met reality. And reality won.

So a new generation of artists started asking a different question. Not "what should the world look like?" but "what does the world actually look like?" Not ideals. Not dreams. Not feelings. Just... what's there.

### Photography

[TALKING HEAD]

And there was another reason to ask that question. Because by 1850, a machine could capture reality.

The daguerreotype had been invented in 1839. By the 1850s, photography was everywhere. And it created an existential crisis for painters. If a machine can show you exactly what something looks like — why paint it?

Realism's answer — and it's a good one — is that a painting isn't a photograph. A photograph records. A painting interprets. A photograph shows you what's there. A painting shows you what it *means*. The camera captures the surface. The painter chooses what to emphasize, what to leave out, what to make you feel.

But the pressure was real. Photography was pushing painting toward a choice: either go inward — toward feeling, abstraction, the subjective — or go deeper into reality than a camera ever could. Realism chose the second path.

### Daumier

[TALKING HEAD]

Before we get to Courbet, I want to show you one image. Because it proves that Realism wasn't just a painting style — it was a way of seeing the world.

[ARTWORK: Daumier, *Rue Transnonain, April 15, 1834*, 8 seconds]

[TALKING HEAD]

This is *Rue Transnonain* by Honoré Daumier. 1834. It's a lithograph — a print, not a painting.

A family has been massacred by government troops in their apartment. A man lies dead in his nightshirt, his body draped over a child. The bed is rumpled. The room is ordinary. There's nothing heroic, nothing dramatic, nothing composed. It's just the aftermath of violence, rendered with devastating plainness.

Daumier was a cartoonist — he published thousands of political caricatures in newspapers. He'd already been imprisoned for six months for drawing the king as Gargantua, gorging himself while the poor fed him. The government took his cartoons seriously because they *worked*.

But *Rue Transnonain* isn't a cartoon. It's journalism. It's as powerful as Géricault's Raft, and it's the size of a newspaper page. Daumier proved you didn't need a massive canvas to tell the truth. You just needed to look.

## ACT 3: COURBET

*[Underscore builds — more energy. We're meeting the main character.]*

### The Persona

[TALKING HEAD]

Gustave Courbet was born in 1819 in Ornans — a small town in the Franche-Comté region of eastern France. His father was a prosperous farmer. Not poor, but not Parisian. And Courbet never let anyone forget where he came from. His whole career was built on being the outsider, the provincial, the man who didn't play by Paris's rules.

He went to Paris in 1839, supposedly to study law. He abandoned law immediately and started painting. He was largely self-taught — he spent hours in the Louvre studying Rembrandt, Velázquez, the Venetians. He rejected academic training. He didn't want to learn the rules. He wanted to break them.

And from the very beginning, he was building a persona. His early self-portraits are fascinating — he paints himself as a romantic hero, a wounded man, a desperate man, a man with a pipe, a man meeting a patron on the road. He's trying on identities like costumes. He was constructing a brand before branding existed.

There's a quote attributed to him — it might be apocryphal, but it's perfect: "Show me an angel and I'll paint one."

*[Beat]*

That's Courbet in six words. If it doesn't exist in the real world, he's not interested.

### The Burial at Ornans

[TALKING HEAD — this is the centerpiece, take your time]

In 1849, Courbet went home to Ornans and started work on the painting that would make him famous and infamous in equal measure.

*A Burial at Ornans.* He asked the people of his hometown to pose for him. The mayor. The priest. The gravedigger. The mourners. Real people, with real faces, in their real clothes. He painted them life-size, on a canvas ten feet tall and twenty-two feet wide.

*[Music drops out.]*

[ARTWORK: *A Burial at Ornans*, full screen]

[SILENCE — 10 to 15 seconds]

[TALKING HEAD — slow, deliberate]

I want you to look at this painting the way Paris saw it in 1850.

[DETAIL: The mourners' faces — unidealized, specific, real]

Look at the faces. These aren't types. They're not "the grieving mother" or "the noble priest." They're specific people. This woman has a red nose. That man looks bored. The dog doesn't care about any of it. Nobody is performing grief for the viewer. They're just... there. At a funeral. The way people actually are at funerals — awkward, tired, distracted, sad in private ways that don't photograph well.

[DETAIL: The grave — the open hole in the ground]

And the grave. It's just a hole. A rectangle of brown earth. No marble. No monument. No symbolism. A hole in the ground where a body is going to go.

[DETAIL: The full width of the painting — the procession stretching across]

Now think about the size. This canvas is twenty-two feet wide. That's the size David used for *The Coronation of Napoleon*. That's the size reserved for the most important events in history — the death of kings, the triumph of empires.

And Courbet used it for a funeral in Ornans.

[TALKING HEAD]

That's the provocation. Not the subject — people had painted funerals before. The *scale*. By painting ordinary people at the scale of history painting, Courbet was making a statement that was, in 1850, genuinely radical: these people matter. Their lives matter. Their deaths matter. As much as any king's. As much as any god's.

The critics understood exactly what he was doing, and they hated it. "Ugly." "Vulgar." "A glorification of the common." One wrote that Courbet had "a deliberate intention to degrade art."

Courbet's response? He kept going.

### The Stone Breakers

[TALKING HEAD]

The same year — 1849 — he painted *The Stone Breakers*.

[ARTWORK: *The Stone Breakers* (photograph of the destroyed painting), 8 seconds]

[TALKING HEAD]

Two men breaking stones on a road. One old, one young. Their faces are turned away from us — we can't see them. They're anonymous. Interchangeable. This isn't a story about two specific people. It's a painting about labor as a condition. This is what these men do. This is what they'll always do. The young one will become the old one. Nothing changes.

Courbet's friend Proudhon — the anarchist philosopher — saw it as a political statement about class. Courbet said he just painted what he saw on the road near Ornans. Both were probably right.

Now, here's something I need to tell you. This painting was destroyed. In February 1945, during the bombing of Dresden, the transport carrying it was hit. The painting burned. We only have black-and-white photographs.

*[Beat]*

There's something almost unbearably poignant about that. A painting about the disposability of workers — about people whose labor is used up and forgotten — was itself used up and forgotten. Destroyed as collateral damage in a war. The painting about anonymous loss became an anonymous loss.

### Millet

[TALKING HEAD — shifting tone, gentler]

Courbet wasn't the only one painting peasants. There was another painter — quieter, less confrontational, but just as important — named Jean-François Millet.

Millet was the real thing. He was actually from a peasant family in Normandy. He didn't have to go looking for his subjects — he'd grown up among them. He settled in Barbizon, a village near the Forest of Fontainebleau, and he painted the people he knew.

[ARTWORK: *The Gleaners*, 8 seconds]

*The Gleaners.* 1857. Three women bending to pick up leftover grain after a harvest. It's backbreaking work — the lowest form of agricultural labor. But Millet paints them like monuments. They fill the canvas. They have the weight and dignity of classical sculpture.

Critics saw it as dangerous. "These are the scarecrows of poverty," one wrote. "Their ugliness is a protest." They accused Millet of being a socialist. He denied it. He said he just painted what he knew.

*[Slight smile]*

Same defense Courbet used. Funny how that works.

[ARTWORK: *The Angelus*, 5 seconds]

And then there's *The Angelus* — a man and woman pausing in a field at sunset to pray. It became one of the most reproduced images in the world. You've seen it on calendars, plates, postcards, probably without knowing what it was. Dalí was so obsessed with it he wrote an entire book arguing that the woman was actually mourning a dead child buried in the field. Which is... very Dalí.

If Courbet is the punk rocker of Realism, Millet is the folk singer. Same subjects. Completely different temperament. Courbet confronts you. Millet invites you in.

## ACT 4: THE PAVILION

*[Underscore — defiant energy.]*

[TALKING HEAD]

In 1855, Paris hosted the Exposition Universelle — the World's Fair. A massive art exhibition was part of it. Courbet submitted fourteen paintings. The jury accepted eleven.

But they rejected the two most important ones: *A Burial at Ornans* and a new painting called *The Painter's Studio*.

Now, most artists would have been angry. Maybe written a letter. Maybe complained to their friends.

Courbet built a building.

*[Beat]*

He literally built his own exhibition hall. Across the street from the official one. He called it the **Pavilion of Realism**. He charged admission. He published a manifesto.

*[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "To create living art — this is my goal." — Courbet, 1855]*

"To create living art — this is my goal."

This is the first time in modern art history that an artist staged a major independent exhibition. Think about what that means. Every independent show that came after — the Impressionist exhibitions, the Salon des Refusés, every artist-run gallery, every DIY show in a warehouse — Courbet was the prototype. He looked at the system that controlled who got seen and who didn't, and he said: I'll build my own.

*[Beat]*

It's the art world equivalent of starting your own label after the record company drops you. Except it's 1855 and nobody had ever done it before.

### The Painter's Studio

[ARTWORK: *The Painter's Studio*, slow pan, 8–10 seconds]

[TALKING HEAD]

And the painting the Salon rejected? *The Painter's Studio: A Real Allegory Summing Up Seven Years of My Artistic and Moral Life.* That's the actual title. Twelve by twenty feet.

Courbet is at the center. Literally at the center of the canvas, painting a landscape. On his left: the world of commonplace life — beggars, workers, a priest, a hunter, a skull on a newspaper. On his right: the world of art — collectors, critics, and — look closely — that's Baudelaire, sitting in the corner, reading a book. Baudelaire is *in the painting*.

Courbet put himself at the center of the world. On one side, reality. On the other, art. And him in the middle, mediating between them.

The arrogance is breathtaking. But here's the thing — it's also kind of justified. Because that *is* what he was doing. He was standing between the real world and the art world and saying: these two things need to talk to each other. Art needs to be about life. And life deserves art.

## ACT 5: THE FALL

*[Underscore shifts — darker, heavier.]*

### The Scandalous Works

[TALKING HEAD]

Before we get to the end of Courbet's story, I need to mention one painting. I probably can't show it to you uncensored, but I need to describe it, because it matters.

In 1866, Courbet painted *The Origin of the World*. It's a close-up painting of a woman's genitals. That's it. No face. No context. No mythological excuse. Just a body, painted frankly, unapologetically, as it is.

It was commissioned by a Turkish-Egyptian diplomat for his private collection. It was hidden from public view for over a century. It now hangs in the Musée d'Orsay in Paris. And it's still startling.

Why does it matter? Because it's the logical endpoint of Courbet's entire project. If you're going to paint reality — all of reality — without idealization, without apology, without the fig leaf of mythology... this is where you end up. The body as it is. Not as the Académie says it should be.

### The Commune

[TALKING HEAD — tone shift, serious]

In 1870, France went to war with Prussia. And lost. Catastrophically. Napoleon III was captured. The Empire collapsed. Paris was besieged for four months. People starved. They ate rats. They ate zoo animals.

And then, in March 1871, something extraordinary happened. The people of Paris — workers, radicals, socialists — seized control of the city and declared the Paris Commune. A radical, democratic, self-governing city. For seventy-two days, Paris was its own country.

Courbet was elected to the Commune's council. He was put in charge of protecting Parisian art and museums — which, given that he was the most famous artist in France, made sense.

But then there was the Vendôme Column.

The Vendôme Column was a monument in central Paris — a tall column topped with a statue of Napoleon, made from melted-down enemy cannons. A symbol of imperial conquest. The Commune voted to demolish it. Courbet supported the demolition. Whether he proposed it or merely went along with it is still debated. But his name was attached.

The column came down on May 16, 1871.

And then, five days later, the French army retook Paris. What followed was called Bloody Week. Between ten and twenty thousand Communards were killed. Mass executions. Mass deportations. It was a massacre.

Courbet was arrested. Imprisoned. Sentenced to six months. And then — the final blow — he was ordered to pay for the reconstruction of the Vendôme Column. Three hundred and twenty-three thousand francs. An impossible sum.

He fled to Switzerland in 1873. Lived in exile near Lake Geneva. He drank heavily. His health deteriorated. The man who had been the loudest, most vital force in French art was falling apart.

He died on New Year's Eve, 1877. In La Tour-de-Peilz, Switzerland. He was fifty-eight. The column debt was still unpaid.

*[Beat]*

There's a parallel to David here that I can't ignore. Both painters got entangled in revolutionary politics. Both ended in exile. Both died in foreign countries.

But there's a crucial difference. David switched allegiances — from the Revolution to Napoleon — without missing a beat. Courbet never did. He stayed a radical to the end. He paid for it with everything.

Near the end of his life, he wrote something that I think is the best epitaph any artist has ever written for themselves:

"I am fifty years old and I have always lived in freedom; let me end my life free; when I am dead let this be said of me: 'He belonged to no school, to no church, to no institution, to no academy, least of all to any régime except the régime of liberty.'"

## ACT 6: THE LEGACY

*[Quiet underscore. Reflective.]*

[TALKING HEAD — direct to camera]

What did Courbet prove?

He proved you can paint real life. Not mythology, not allegory, not the dreams of the ruling class — just life. Ugly, ordinary, unglamorous life. And he proved that it's worthy of the biggest canvas you can find.

He proved you can reject the system. You can build your own exhibition hall. You can publish your own manifesto. You can tell the Salon and the Académie and the entire art establishment to go to hell, and you can survive it. Maybe not comfortably. Maybe not happily. But you can do it.

Every independent exhibition that came after him — the Impressionists in 1874, the Secessionists in Vienna, every artist-run space, every punk who printed their own zine — Courbet was there first.

There's a short story by Balzac called *The Unknown Masterpiece* — about a painter who chases reality so obsessively that he destroys his own painting. He works and reworks it until there's nothing left but a chaos of color with one perfect foot emerging from the mess. Courbet knew that story. He was determined not to make the same mistake — to chase truth without losing the painting.

Cézanne, in Episode 5, will identify with that story on a much deeper level. But that's later.

*[Beat. Energy builds.]*

Courbet proved you could paint real life. But a group of young painters — some of whom attended Delacroix's funeral, some of whom had seen Courbet's work and been electrified by it — were about to ask a question Courbet never thought to ask.

Not "what is real life?" but "what does real life actually *look like*?"

Not what's in front of you. But how your eyes actually *see* it. The light. The color. The way a face dissolves in afternoon sun. The way water shimmers. The way a moment — a single, unrepeatable moment — feels before it's gone.

They'd be mocked. They'd be rejected. They'd be named after an insult. And they'd change everything.

That's next time.

*[Cut to black.]*
