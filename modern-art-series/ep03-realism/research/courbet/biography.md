# Gustave Courbet (1819–1877)

## The Short Version
The loudest, most arrogant, most brilliant troublemaker in 19th-century art. He painted peasants and funerals at the scale reserved for gods and kings. He built his own exhibition hall when the establishment rejected him. He helped destroy a national monument during the Paris Commune and died in exile. He is the father of Realism.

## Life Timeline

### Origins (1819–1843)
- Born June 10, 1819, in Ornans, a small town in the Franche-Comté region of eastern France. This matters — he never stopped being a provincial. His art is rooted in this landscape and these people.
- Son of a prosperous farmer. Not poor, but not Parisian.
- Went to Paris in 1839 to study law. Immediately abandoned law for painting.
- Largely self-taught. Studied the Old Masters in the Louvre obsessively — especially Rembrandt, Velázquez, and the Venetians. Rejected academic training.
- Early self-portraits show a young man performing different roles: romantic hero, wounded man, desperate man. He was constructing a persona from the start.

### The Breakthrough (1848–1850)
- The Revolution of 1848 radicalized him. The overthrow of the monarchy, the brief Second Republic, the workers' uprising — Courbet was in Paris for all of it.
- **After Dinner at Ornans** (1849) — A large-scale painting of four men sitting around a table after a meal. Ordinary people. Ordinary moment. Painted at the scale of a history painting. The Salon accepted it and gave him a medal. But critics were confused: why is this so *big*?
- **A Burial at Ornans** (1849–1850) — THE painting. 10 × 22 feet. A funeral in his hometown. Real people from Ornans posed for it — the mayor, the priest, the gravedigger, mourners. No idealization. No moral lesson. No heroes. Just a funeral. With ugly people. And a dog.
  - The Salon displayed it, and Paris exploded. Critics called it "ugly," "vulgar," "a glorification of the common." One wrote that it was "a masquerade funeral six meters long in which there is more to laugh at than to weep over."
  - Courbet's response, essentially: yes, and?
  - This painting is the birth certificate of Realism. It says: ordinary life, at this scale, is worthy of art. That's a revolutionary statement.

### The Stone Breakers (1849)
- Two men breaking stones on a road. One old, one young. Their faces are turned away — they're anonymous, interchangeable. This is labor as a condition, not a story.
- Destroyed in the bombing of Dresden in 1945. We only have photographs and reproductions.
- Proudhon (the anarchist philosopher, Courbet's friend) saw it as a political statement about class. Courbet said he just painted what he saw. Both were probably right.

### The Pavilion of Realism (1855)
- The 1855 Exposition Universelle (World's Fair) in Paris. A massive art exhibition was part of it. Courbet submitted 14 paintings. The jury accepted 11 but rejected his two most important: *A Burial at Ornans* and *The Painter's Studio*.
- Courbet's response: **he built his own exhibition hall** across the street from the official one. Called it the "Pavilion of Realism." Charged admission. Published a manifesto.
- This is the first time an artist staged a major independent exhibition. It's the prototype for everything from the Impressionist exhibitions to modern gallery shows.
- The manifesto declared: "The title of Realist was thrust upon me just as the title of Romantic was thrust upon the men of 1830. Titles have never given a true idea of things: if it were otherwise, the works would be unnecessary."

### The Painter's Studio (1855)
- Full title: *The Painter's Studio: A Real Allegory Summing Up Seven Years of My Artistic and Moral Life*
- Enormous (12 × 20 feet). Courbet at the center, painting a landscape. On one side: "the world of commonplace life" (beggars, workers, a priest, a hunter). On the other: "the world of art" (Baudelaire reading, a nude model, collectors).
- It's a manifesto in paint. Courbet literally put himself at the center of the world.
- The arrogance is breathtaking. It's also kind of justified.

### The Scandalous Works
- **The Origin of the World** (1866) — A close-up painting of a woman's genitals. Commissioned by a Turkish-Egyptian diplomat for his private collection. It's frank, unapologetic, and was hidden from public view for over a century. Now hangs in the Musée d'Orsay. It's still startling.
- **The Sleepers** (1866) — Two women in bed together, post-coital. Same commissioner. Courbet painted bodies as they are, not as the Académie said they should be.

### The Paris Commune & Downfall (1870–1877)
- Franco-Prussian War (1870–1871). France defeated. Napoleon III falls. Paris besieged.
- The Paris Commune (March–May 1871) — A radical socialist government takes control of Paris for 72 days.
- Courbet was elected to the Commune's council. He was put in charge of protecting Parisian art and museums.
- **The Vendôme Column:** A monument topped with a statue of Napoleon, made from melted-down enemy cannons. The Commune voted to demolish it as a symbol of imperialism. Courbet supported the demolition (though his exact role is debated — he may have proposed dismantling rather than destroying it).
- The Commune was crushed. Thousands killed. Courbet arrested.
- Sentenced to six months in prison and ordered to pay for the reconstruction of the column — a debt of 323,000 francs (an impossible sum).
- Fled to Switzerland in 1873. Lived in exile near Lake Geneva.
- Drank heavily. Health deteriorated.
- Died December 31, 1877, in La Tour-de-Peilz, Switzerland. He was 58. The column debt was still unpaid.

## Key Themes for the Episode
1. **The dignity of the ordinary** — Courbet's radical claim: a funeral in Ornans is as worthy of monumental painting as the death of a Greek hero
2. **The artist as self-promoter** — He invented the modern artist persona: loud, confrontational, media-savvy. The Pavilion of Realism is the first art-world publicity stunt.
3. **Art and politics** — Unlike David (who served power) or Delacroix (who painted the feeling of revolution), Courbet was a genuine political radical who paid for it with exile and death
4. **"Ugly" truth vs. "beautiful" lies** — The central tension. Is honest ugliness more valuable than idealized beauty?

## Quotes to Use
- "I have studied the art of the ancients and the art of the moderns, avoiding any preconceived system and without prejudice. I no longer wanted to imitate the one than to copy the other; nor, furthermore, was it my intention to attain the trivial goal of 'art for art's sake.'"
- "Show me an angel and I'll paint one." (Attributed — possibly apocryphal, but perfectly captures his philosophy)
- "The title of Realist was thrust upon me just as the title of Romantic was thrust upon the men of 1830."
- "I am fifty years old and I have always lived in freedom; let me end my life free; when I am dead let this be said of me: 'He belonged to no school, to no church, to no institution, to no academy, least of all to any régime except the régime of liberty.'"
- On the Burial at Ornans: "The people of Ornans... came to pose for me with the best will in the world."
