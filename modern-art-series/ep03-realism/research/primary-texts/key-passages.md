# Primary Texts & Key Passages — Episode 3: Realism

---

## 1. Courbet — The Realist Manifesto (1855)

Published as the preface to the catalogue of his Pavilion of Realism exhibition.

### Key Passages
> "The title of Realist was thrust upon me just as the title of Romantic was thrust upon the men of 1830. Titles have never given a true idea of things: if it were otherwise, the works would be unnecessary."

> "I have studied the art of the ancients and the art of the moderns, avoiding any preconceived system and without prejudice. I no longer wanted to imitate the one than to copy the other; nor, furthermore, was it my intention to attain the trivial goal of 'art for art's sake.' No! I simply wanted to draw forth, from a complete acquaintance with tradition, the reasoned and independent consciousness of my own individuality."

> "To know in order to do, that was my idea. To be in a position to translate the customs, the ideas, the appearance of my epoch, according to my own estimation; to be not only a painter, but a man as well; in short, to create living art — this is my goal."

### Why This Matters
This is the mission statement. "To create living art" — art that's about *now*, about real people, about the actual world. Put "to create living art" on screen.

**Source:** Widely reprinted. Available in Holt, Holt & Holt, *Art in Theory 1815–1900* and many online sources.

## 2. Balzac — "The Unknown Masterpiece" (1831)

### Why It's Here
A short story about a painter who spends years perfecting a single painting, convinced it's his masterpiece. When he finally reveals it, his fellow painters see nothing but a chaos of colors with one perfect foot emerging from the mess. The painter has destroyed his own work through obsessive pursuit of perfection.

### Relevance to the Episode
- It's a meditation on the gap between artistic ambition and reality — a theme that runs through Courbet's career.
- Courbet knew this story. Cézanne later identified with it deeply (connects to Episode 5).
- It asks: when does the pursuit of truth in painting become madness?

### Key Passage
> "There is no such thing as a line in nature, where everything is full. It is in modelling that one draws, that is to say, one detaches things from their surroundings; the distribution of light alone gives the appearance of the body."

### For the Script
You don't need to summarize the whole story. Just mention it as a literary parallel — "Balzac wrote a story about a painter who chased reality so hard he lost it entirely. Courbet was determined not to make the same mistake."

**Source:** Available free on Project Gutenberg and many other sites. Short — can be read in one sitting.

## 3. Proudhon — "On the Principle of Art and Its Social Destination" (1865)

### Why It Matters
Pierre-Joseph Proudhon was an anarchist philosopher and Courbet's close friend. This book (published posthumously) argues that art must serve social justice. He uses Courbet's paintings as his primary examples.

### Key Idea
Art that doesn't engage with social reality is decadent and useless. The purpose of art is to reveal truth and inspire change.

### Useful Passage
> "To paint men in the sincerity of their natures and their habits, in their work, in the accomplishment of their civic and domestic functions, with their present-day appearance... that would be the true painting."

### For the Script
Proudhon gives you a way to articulate the political dimension of Realism without it being purely your own analysis. "Courbet's friend, the anarchist philosopher Proudhon, put it simply: paint people as they actually are. That's the revolution."

## 4. Champfleury — "Realism" (1857)

### Who He Was
Jules Champfleury was a writer and critic who was Courbet's main intellectual ally. He coined and defended the term "Realism" in art criticism.

### Key Idea
> "The reproduction of nature by man will never be a reproduction or an imitation; it will always be an interpretation... since man is not a machine and is incapable of rendering objects mechanically."

### Why This Matters
This directly addresses the photography question. A painting isn't a photograph. Even when a Realist paints "what's there," they're interpreting, selecting, emphasizing. Realism isn't mechanical reproduction — it's a point of view.
