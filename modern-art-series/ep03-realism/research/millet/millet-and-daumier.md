# Jean-François Millet (1814–1875)

## Role in the Episode
The quiet counterpart to Courbet's loudness. If Courbet is the punk rocker of Realism, Millet is the folk singer. He painted peasants too, but with a reverence and solemnity that Courbet would have found sentimental. Together they represent two faces of the same movement.

## Key Facts
- Born into a peasant family in Normandy. He actually *was* the people he painted (unlike Courbet, who was a prosperous farmer's son playing the rebel).
- Moved to Paris, struggled, eventually settled in Barbizon (a village near the Forest of Fontainebleau) in 1849. Lived there for the rest of his life.
- The Barbizon School: a loose group of painters (Millet, Rousseau, Corot, Daubigny) who painted directly from nature. They're a bridge between Romanticism and Impressionism.

## Key Works
- **The Gleaners** (1857) — Three women bending to pick up leftover grain after a harvest. Backbreaking labor. The women are monumental — they fill the canvas like classical figures. But they're poor. Critics saw it as dangerously socialist: "These are the scarecrows of poverty... their ugliness is a protest."
- **The Angelus** (1857–1859) — A man and woman pausing in a field at sunset to pray. A church steeple in the distance. It became one of the most reproduced images in the world — you've seen it on calendars, plates, postcards. Dalí was obsessed with it (he thought the woman was actually mourning a dead child buried in the field).
- **Man with a Hoe** (1862) — A peasant leaning on his hoe, exhausted. Inspired a famous poem by Edwin Markham.

## What to Emphasize
- The contrast with Courbet: same subjects, completely different attitude. Courbet is confrontational. Millet is empathetic.
- Millet was accused of being a socialist. He denied it. He said he just painted what he knew. (Same defense Courbet used.)
- Brief — 2–3 minutes. He's the secondary voice.

# Honoré Daumier (1808–1879)

## Role in the Episode
The cartoonist who became a painter. Daumier proves that Realism wasn't just about oil painting — it was a way of seeing that crossed media. His lithographs are some of the sharpest political satire ever made.

## Key Facts
- Born in Marseille, 1808. Working-class family. Moved to Paris as a child.
- Became a lithographer and caricaturist. Published thousands of prints in newspapers and magazines.
- **Imprisoned for six months in 1832** for a caricature of King Louis-Philippe as Gargantua (the Rabelais character) — shown gorging himself while the poor feed him. The government took political cartoons seriously because they *worked*.
- His most famous print: *Rue Transnonain, April 15, 1834* — A family massacred by government troops in their apartment. It's not a cartoon — it's journalism. It's as powerful as Géricault's Raft.
- Later in life, turned to painting and sculpture. His paintings of washerwomen, third-class railway carriages, and Don Quixote are remarkable — loose, empathetic, modern.
- Went blind in his later years. Died in poverty in 1879.

## What to Emphasize
- The *Rue Transnonain* print — show it. It's devastating and it connects Realism to political protest.
- Daumier as proof that Realism was a *worldview*, not just a painting style.
- Very brief — 1–2 minutes. But the Rue Transnonain image is worth showing.
