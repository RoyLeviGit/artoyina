# Historical Context: Realism (~1848–1880)

## What Was Happening in the World

### The 1848 Revolutions
- February 1848: Revolution in France. Louis-Philippe overthrown. The Second Republic declared.
- The revolution spread across Europe: Vienna, Berlin, Milan, Budapest, Prague. The "Springtime of Peoples."
- In France, the June Days uprising (1848): workers vs. the government. Brutally suppressed. Thousands killed or deported.
- The lesson many artists took: the Romantic dream of revolution leads to bloodshed and betrayal. Time to look at reality without illusions.
- Louis-Napoleon (Napoleon's nephew) elected president in 1848. Staged a coup in 1851. Declared himself Emperor Napoleon III in 1852. The Second Empire begins.

### The Second Empire (1852–1870)
- Napoleon III modernizes France. Baron Haussmann redesigns Paris: wide boulevards, new buildings, the Paris we know today.
- But modernization has a cost: working-class neighborhoods demolished, the poor pushed to the margins.
- Rapid industrialization. The gap between rich and poor widens.
- This is the world Courbet, Millet, and Daumier are painting: the reality behind the glittering facade.

### The Franco-Prussian War & the Commune (1870–1871)
- France declares war on Prussia. Catastrophic defeat. Napoleon III captured. The Empire collapses.
- Paris besieged for four months. Starvation. People ate rats and zoo animals.
- The Paris Commune (March–May 1871): a radical socialist government seizes Paris. 72 days of self-rule.
- The French army retakes Paris in "Bloody Week" (May 21–28, 1871). Between 10,000 and 20,000 Communards killed. Mass executions. Mass deportations.
- Courbet was in the middle of all of this.

### The Rise of Photography
- Daguerreotype invented in 1839. By the 1850s, photography is widespread.
- This changes everything for painters. If a machine can capture reality, what's the point of painting it?
- Realism's answer: painting isn't just recording — it's *interpreting*. A photograph shows you what's there. A painting shows you what it *means*.
- But the pressure is real. Photography pushes painting toward either greater subjectivity (→ Impressionism, Post-Impressionism) or greater social commentary (→ Realism).

## The Art World Context

### The Salon System Under Pressure
- Still dominant, but increasingly contested.
- The jury is conservative, politically connected, and hostile to anything that challenges the hierarchy of genres.
- Courbet's Pavilion of Realism (1855) = the first major crack. An artist saying: I don't need your approval.
- The Salon des Refusés (1863) = Napoleon III, embarrassed by the number of rejections, orders a separate exhibition of rejected works. Manet's *Olympia* and *Le Déjeuner sur l'herbe* are shown there. This directly sets up Episode 4.

### The Hierarchy of Genres (Still)
- History painting still officially at the top. But Courbet's whole project is to demolish this hierarchy.
- Painting a funeral at the scale of a history painting = saying: this matters as much as the death of Caesar.
- Painting stone breakers = saying: labor is as worthy a subject as mythology.

## The Big Question for the Episode
Is "ugly" truth more valuable than "beautiful" lies?

Courbet forces the question. His paintings aren't pretty. They're not uplifting. They don't teach a moral lesson. They just show you what's there. And the art establishment's furious reaction proves how threatening that simple act was.
