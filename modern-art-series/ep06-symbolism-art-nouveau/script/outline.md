# Episode 6 Outline: "The Dreamers and the Decorators"
## Movements: Symbolism + Art Nouveau | Runtime Target: 40–50 min

---

### 1. COLD OPEN (2–3 min)
**Scene:** Vienna, 1900. In one building, a doctor is writing a book about dreams. In another, a composer is conducting an opera about death and resurrection. And in a white building with a golden dome that the locals call "the golden cabbage," a painter is covering an entire wall with gold leaf, naked bodies, and a knight in armor — all in honor of Beethoven.

The doctor is Freud. The composer is Mahler. The painter is Gustav Klimt. And this city, in this moment, is the most creatively explosive place on earth.

But to understand how we got here, we need to go back to the 1880s — to a group of artists who turned away from the visible world entirely and looked inward. Into dreams. Into myth. Into the dark.

---

### 2. "OKAY, LET ME BACK UP" — The Symbolist Turn (5–8 min)

**The problem after Realism and Impressionism:**
- Realism said: paint what's there. Impressionism said: paint what you see. Post-Impressionism pushed further.
- But some artists asked: what about what you *can't* see? What about dreams, fears, desires, the unconscious?
- The Symbolists turned inward. Their subjects: mythology, death, sexuality, the spiritual, the irrational.

**Moreau — the reclusive professor:**
- *The Apparition*: Salome and the floating head of John the Baptist. Show it. Erotic, morbid, hypnotic.
- Moreau painted elaborate mythological fantasies. Every inch packed with detail.
- The irony: his students included Matisse. The most conservative painter in Paris accidentally trained the most radical painters of the next century.

**Redon — the man who lived twice:**
- The Noirs: decades of black-and-white nightmares. Floating eyeballs, smiling spiders, strange creatures.
- Then, around 50, he exploded into color. Flowers, butterflies, radiant mythological scenes.
- Show the before and after. The contrast is the story.
- *The Cyclops*: a one-eyed giant in love. Tender, luminous, strange.

**What Symbolism was:**
- Not a style but an attitude. Art should suggest, not describe. It should evoke moods, not tell stories.
- The literary connection: Mallarmé, Verlaine, Huysmans. Poets who valued suggestion over statement.

---

### 3. THE MAIN CHARACTER — Klimt (12–15 min)

**Early success:**
- Son of a gold engraver. Trained in applied arts, not fine arts. Never saw a line between decoration and art.
- Early career: conventional, successful, decorative painting for public buildings.
- His brother dies in 1892. Everything begins to shift.

**The university paintings scandal:**
- Commissioned to paint *Philosophy*, *Medicine*, *Jurisprudence* for the University of Vienna.
- They expected classical allegories. He delivered tangled naked bodies, death, suffering, sexuality.
- 87 professors signed a petition. The press attacked. Politicians intervened.
- "Enough of censorship. I want to get free."
- He withdrew the paintings. They were later destroyed by the Nazis. We only have photographs.
- This is the David parallel from Episode 1, inverted: David served the state with his art. Klimt refused to.

**The Vienna Secession:**
- 1897: Klimt and allies break away from the conservative establishment.
- "To every age its art, to every art its freedom." Put it on screen.
- The Secession Building: the "golden cabbage." Show it.
- **The Beethoven Frieze**: 112 feet of allegory. Hostile forces, the golden knight, the final embrace. "This kiss to the whole world."

**The Golden Phase:**
- *Portrait of Adele Bloch-Bauer I*: the Woman in Gold. Face and hands are real; everything else is gold and pattern. The person dissolves into decoration. Or: decoration becomes as real as the person.
- *The Kiss*: the most iconic image. Tenderness, eroticism, gold. Two people wrapped in pattern, kneeling on a cliff of flowers.
- The gold isn't just pretty. It's Byzantine — sacred art. It's also erotic. Klimt collapses the distance between the icon and the pinup. That's his genius.

**Emilie Flögé:**
- Fashion designer. His lifelong companion. She destroyed their letters.
- Last words: "Send for Emilie."

---

### 4. ART NOUVEAU — Beauty Everywhere (8–10 min)

**The idea:**
- What if art wasn't just in museums? What if it was in your building, your furniture, your poster, your jewelry, your subway entrance?
- Art Nouveau wanted to dissolve the boundary between fine art and daily life.

**The Arts and Crafts precursor:**
- William Morris: handmade beauty as a protest against industrial ugliness.
- Art Nouveau inherited the idea but embraced mass production. Mucha's posters were printed by the thousands.

**Mucha:**
- Show the posters. Flowing hair, floral borders, sinuous lines. This is the graphic design of 1900.

**Gaudí:**
- Show the architecture. Sagrada Família, Casa Batlló, Park Güell.
- No straight lines. Everything organic, flowing, alive.
- He was hit by a tram and died unrecognized. The architect of the most extraordinary buildings in Europe looked like a beggar.

**Horta & the whiplash curve:**
- The Hôtel Tassel staircase. Iron as decoration. Structure as beauty.

**Art Nouveau across Europe:**
- Different names, same impulse: Art Nouveau, Jugendstil, Stile Liberty, Modernisme, Glasgow Style.
- Map on screen showing the spread.

---

### 5. THE BACKLASH — Ornament and Crime (3–5 min)

**Adolf Loos:**
- "The evolution of culture is synonymous with the removal of ornament."
- He looked at Art Nouveau and saw waste, excess, decadence.
- His essay predicted the entire 20th century of design: clean lines, no decoration, "less is more."

**Louis Sullivan:**
- "Form ever follows function."
- The American parallel. A building should express its purpose, not disguise it.

**The tension:**
- Art Nouveau says: make everything beautiful.
- Loos says: beauty IS simplicity. Ornament is a crime.
- This argument is still happening. Every time someone debates minimalism vs. maximalism, they're replaying Klimt vs. Loos.

---

### 6. THE CLOSING — The Century Turns (3–5 min)

**What happened:**
- By 1900, art had been through a century of revolution. It broke free from the academy, from the church, from the state.
- Neo-Classicism said: serve reason. Romanticism said: serve emotion. Realism said: serve truth. Impressionism said: serve the eye. Post-Impressionism said: serve the self. Symbolism said: serve the soul. Art Nouveau said: serve life.

**The big question (direct to camera):**
- Should art serve the soul or serve daily life? Klimt's answer: yes. Both. At the same time. Gold and sex and death and beauty, on a wall, in a building, in a poster, in a kiss.

**Closing:**
> "By 1900, art had been through a century of revolution. It had broken free from the academy, from the church, from the state. But nobody — *nobody* — was ready for what was about to happen next. The 20th century was coming. Picasso was 19 years old. Matisse was 30. Duchamp was 13. And they were going to make everything we've talked about in this series look polite."

> "But that's another story."
