# Gustav Klimt (1862–1918)

## The Short Version
The man who painted gold and sex in Vienna, scandalized an entire empire, and created some of the most recognizable images in art history. He bridges Symbolism and Art Nouveau — his work is both deeply symbolic (dreams, death, sexuality) and stunningly decorative (gold leaf, ornamental patterns, mosaic-like surfaces). He's the central figure of this episode because he embodies both movements.

## Life Timeline

### Early Career & the Establishment (1862–1897)
- Born July 14, 1862, in Baumgarten, near Vienna. Son of a gold engraver (this matters — gold was literally in his blood).
- Trained at the Vienna School of Arts and Crafts. Not the fine arts academy — the applied arts school. This shaped his approach: he never saw a hard line between "fine art" and "decorative art."
- Early career: hugely successful as a decorative painter. Commissions for theaters, museums, public buildings. He painted ceilings, stairwells, curtains. Technically brilliant in a conventional way.
- His brother Ernst and their partner Franz Matsch worked with him as a team. Ernst died in 1892. Klimt was devastated. His work began to change.

### The University Paintings Scandal (1894–1905)
- Commissioned to paint three ceiling panels for the Great Hall of the University of Vienna: *Philosophy*, *Medicine*, and *Jurisprudence*.
- The university expected classical allegories — noble figures representing noble ideas.
- Klimt delivered something completely different: tangled naked bodies, suffering, sexuality, death. *Philosophy* showed a column of intertwined nude figures drifting through a void. *Medicine* featured a skeleton (Death) among the naked bodies. *Jurisprudence* depicted a man being consumed by tentacle-like forms.
- The university faculty was outraged. 87 professors signed a petition against the paintings. The press attacked him. Politicians got involved.
- Klimt withdrew the paintings and returned his fee. He said: "Enough of censorship. I want to get free."
- The paintings were later seized by the Nazis and destroyed in a fire at Schloss Immendorf in 1945. We only have black-and-white photographs. One of the great losses in art history.

### The Vienna Secession (1897–1905)
- In 1897, Klimt and a group of artists broke away from the conservative Künstlerhaus (the Viennese equivalent of the Salon) and founded the **Vienna Secession**.
- Their motto: **"To every age its art, to every art its freedom."** (*Der Zeit ihre Kunst, der Kunst ihre Freiheit.*)
- They built their own exhibition hall — the Secession Building, designed by Joseph Maria Olbrich. It's still standing. A white cube topped with a golden dome of laurel leaves. The Viennese called it "the golden cabbage."
- Klimt was the first president of the Secession.
- **The Beethoven Frieze** (1902) — A monumental wall painting for the Secession's 14th exhibition, dedicated to Beethoven. It's an allegory of the human search for happiness: hostile forces (disease, madness, death), the knight in golden armor, and the final embrace — "This kiss to the whole world" (from Beethoven's Ninth Symphony / Schiller's "Ode to Joy"). It's 112 feet long. It was meant to be temporary. It survived.

### The Golden Phase (1903–1909)
- This is the Klimt everyone knows.
- **Portrait of Adele Bloch-Bauer I** (1907) — "The Woman in Gold." Adele dissolves into a sea of gold leaf and geometric patterns. Her face and hands are realistic; everything else is pure decoration. The boundary between person and ornament disappears.
  - This painting was looted by the Nazis, fought over in court for decades, and eventually returned to the Bloch-Bauer heirs. It sold for $135 million in 2006 — at the time, the most expensive painting ever sold.
- **The Kiss** (1907–08) — A couple embracing on a cliff of flowers, wrapped in golden robes. The man's robe has rectangular patterns; the woman's has circular ones. It's tender, erotic, and iconic. Probably the most reproduced painting after the Mona Lisa and Starry Night.
- **Danaë** (1907), **Water Serpents**, **The Tree of Life** — all from this period. Gold, pattern, sexuality, myth.

### Late Work & Death (1910–1918)
- After 1909, the gold recedes. The late paintings are more colorful, more influenced by Matisse and the Fauves.
- Landscapes: lush, almost abstract gardens and lake scenes. Underrated.
- Died February 6, 1918, in Vienna, from a stroke following the Spanish flu. He was 55.
- His last words, reportedly: "Send for Emilie." (Emilie Flögé, his lifelong companion.)

### Emilie Flögé
- Fashion designer. Klimt's closest relationship for over 20 years. Whether they were lovers is debated — they may have been, or it may have been a deep platonic bond. Klimt had numerous affairs and fathered at least 14 children with various women.
- Flögé ran a fashion salon in Vienna. Klimt designed fabrics for her. Their relationship blurred art, fashion, and life.
- She destroyed their correspondence after his death. We'll never know the full story.

## Key Themes
1. **Art vs. the state** — The university paintings scandal: what happens when an artist refuses to give the establishment what it wants
2. **Gold as language** — Klimt's gold isn't just decorative. It's Byzantine, it's sacred, it's erotic. It collapses the distance between icon and pinup.
3. **The Secession** — Artists building their own institution. The direct descendant of Courbet's Pavilion and the Impressionist exhibitions.
4. **Eros and Thanatos** — Sex and death, intertwined. This is Vienna, Freud's city. Klimt paints what Freud theorizes.

## Quotes to Use
- "Enough of censorship. I want to get free."
- "All art is erotic." (From Adolf Loos, but often associated with the Klimt circle)
- Vienna Secession motto: "To every age its art, to every art its freedom."
- Last words: "Send for Emilie."
- Klimt was famously private and left very few written statements. His art speaks for him.
