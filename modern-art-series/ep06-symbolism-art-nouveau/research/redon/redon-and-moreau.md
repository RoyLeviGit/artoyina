# Odilon Redon (1840–1916)

## Role in the Episode
The Symbolist who lived two lives. For decades he made black-and-white nightmares — charcoal drawings and lithographs of floating eyeballs, strange plants, and melancholy faces. Then, around age 50, he suddenly exploded into color. The transformation is one of the most dramatic in art history.

## Key Facts
- Born April 20, 1840, in Bordeaux. Sickly, isolated childhood. Sent to live with an uncle on a rural estate. The loneliness and the landscape shaped his imagination.
- Studied briefly under Gérôme (academic painter) in Paris. Hated it. Returned to Bordeaux.
- Met the printmaker Rodolphe Bresdin, who introduced him to the world of visionary, fantastical imagery. This was the real education.

## The Noirs (1870s–1890s)
- For over 20 years, Redon worked almost exclusively in black and white: charcoal drawings and lithographic prints.
- He called them his "Noirs" (Blacks).
- Subjects: a giant eyeball floating like a hot-air balloon, a smiling spider, a cyclops gazing at a sleeping woman, strange hybrid creatures, severed heads.
- Influenced by: Edgar Allan Poe, Flaubert's *The Temptation of Saint Anthony*, Darwin (the idea of evolution producing strange forms), microscopy (he was fascinated by what you could see under a microscope).
- The Symbolist writers (Mallarmé, Huysmans) championed him. In Huysmans's novel *À Rebours* (1884), the decadent protagonist collects Redon's prints.

## The Color Explosion (1890s–1916)
- Around 1890, Redon began working in color — pastels, then oils. The shift was dramatic.
- The dark, anxious imagery gave way to radiant flowers, butterflies, mythological scenes bathed in light.
- Why the change? Multiple factors: his son's birth (1889), the death of his brother, a period of personal crisis followed by resolution. He said he "emerged from the shadows."
- **The Cyclops** (~1914) — A one-eyed giant peering over a hillside at a reclining nude. It's tender, not threatening. The monster is in love. Painted in luminous color.
- His flower paintings and pastels are extraordinary — explosions of color that anticipate abstract art.

## What to Emphasize
- The before/after is the story. Show the Noirs, then show the color work. The contrast is stunning.
- He's the Symbolist who proves the movement wasn't just about darkness — it was about the inner world, which includes joy as well as nightmare.
- 3–5 minutes. The visual transformation carries the narrative.

# Gustave Moreau (1826–1898)

## Role in the Episode
The reclusive professor whose students destroyed everything he stood for. Moreau painted elaborate mythological fantasies dripping with jewels, gold, and sexual tension. Then he taught a generation of students — including Matisse and Rouault — who went on to invent Fauvism, the most radical color movement in history. The irony is delicious.

## Key Facts
- Born April 6, 1826, in Paris. Wealthy family. Never had to worry about money.
- Studied under the Romantic painter Chassériau (a student of Ingres — the lineage goes back).
- Traveled to Italy (1857–59). Studied Renaissance masters obsessively. Copied hundreds of works.
- Became increasingly reclusive. Lived with his mother until her death. Had a long, secretive relationship with Alexandrine Dureux.

## Key Works
- **Jupiter and Semele** (1895) — Jupiter in all his divine glory, surrounded by a hallucinatory cascade of figures, jewels, and architectural fragments. Semele, a mortal, is destroyed by the sight of the god in his true form. The painting is overwhelming — every inch is packed with detail. It's like a fever dream of mythology.
- **The Apparition** (1876) — Salome dancing before the floating, luminous head of John the Baptist. The head glows. Blood drips. Salome reaches toward it. It's erotic, morbid, and hypnotic. Huysmans wrote pages about it in *À Rebours*.
- **Oedipus and the Sphinx** (1864) — His breakthrough. Oedipus and the Sphinx face to face, almost like lovers. The monster is beautiful. The hero is passive. The sexual tension is palpable.

## The Teaching Legacy
- Appointed professor at the École des Beaux-Arts in 1892.
- Unlike most academic teachers, he encouraged his students to find their own way. He told them to look at color, to trust their instincts.
- His students included: **Henri Matisse**, **Georges Rouault**, **Albert Marquet**, **Charles Camoin** — the core of what would become Fauvism.
- The irony: Moreau painted meticulous, jewel-encrusted mythological fantasies. His students would paint with wild, uncontrolled color and zero mythological content. He gave them permission to be free, and they used that freedom to destroy his world.
- Moreau's house in Paris is now a museum (Musée Gustave Moreau). He designed it himself as a shrine to his own work. It's extraordinary — floor-to-ceiling paintings in a spiral staircase.

## What to Emphasize
- The teaching irony is the hook. "The most conservative painter in Paris accidentally trained the most radical painters of the next century."
- *The Apparition* is the key image — it's the Symbolist aesthetic in one painting.
- Brief — 2–3 minutes. But the Matisse connection is the punchline.
