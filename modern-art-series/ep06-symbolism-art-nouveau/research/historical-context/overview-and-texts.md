# Art Nouveau Artists & Architecture — Episode 6

## Alphonse Mucha (1860–1939)
- Czech artist working in Paris. Became famous overnight when he designed a poster for Sarah Bernhardt's play *Gismonda* (1894).
- His posters define the Art Nouveau look: flowing hair, floral borders, sinuous lines, beautiful women surrounded by decorative patterns.
- The "Mucha style" became so popular it was essentially the graphic design of the 1890s–1900s.
- Later devoted himself to the *Slav Epic* — 20 enormous paintings on Slavic history. A completely different project.
- For the episode: show 2–3 posters. He's the visual shorthand for Art Nouveau. 1 minute.

## Aubrey Beardsley (1872–1898)
- English illustrator. Died of tuberculosis at 25.
- Black-and-white ink drawings of extraordinary precision and perversity. His illustrations for Oscar Wilde's *Salome* are masterpieces of erotic grotesquerie.
- Influenced by Japanese prints (again) and by the Pre-Raphaelites.
- Scandalous in his time — the drawings are sexually explicit, darkly humorous, and technically flawless.
- For the episode: show a Salome illustration. The contrast with Moreau's *Apparition* (same subject, completely different treatment) could be a nice visual beat. 30 seconds.

## Antoni Gaudí (1852–1926)
- Catalan architect. The most extreme Art Nouveau architect — though he transcends any single label.
- **Sagrada Família** (begun 1882, still unfinished) — A basilica in Barcelona that looks like it grew from the earth. Organic forms, no straight lines, surfaces encrusted with sculpture and color.
- **Casa Batlló** (1904–06) — An apartment building that looks like it's made of bones and scales. The facade undulates. The roof looks like a dragon's back.
- **Park Güell** (1900–14) — A public park with mosaic benches, gingerbread houses, and a mosaic salamander.
- Gaudí was deeply religious and increasingly ascetic. He was hit by a tram in 1926 and died because no one recognized him — he looked like a beggar.
- For the episode: show the architecture. It speaks for itself. 1–2 minutes. The visual impact is the argument.

## Victor Horta (1861–1947)
- Belgian architect. Designed the first true Art Nouveau building: the Hôtel Tassel in Brussels (1893).
- Iron and glass used as decorative elements. Whiplash curves. Every detail designed — door handles, stair rails, light fixtures.
- Art Nouveau's argument: there should be no boundary between architecture and decoration, between structure and beauty.
- For the episode: brief mention + photo of Hôtel Tassel staircase. The iron curves are iconic.

# Historical Context: Symbolism + Art Nouveau (~1885–1910)

## Vienna 1900
- One of the most extraordinary concentrations of genius in history:
  - **Freud** publishing *The Interpretation of Dreams* (1900)
  - **Klimt** painting gold and sex
  - **Mahler** directing the Vienna Court Opera
  - **Schiele** and **Kokoschka** emerging as the next generation
  - **Wittgenstein** growing up in one of the city's wealthiest families
  - **Adolf Loos** writing against ornament
- The Austro-Hungarian Empire is in its twilight. The surface is glittering; underneath, everything is cracking. Klimt's art captures this perfectly — beauty and decay, eros and thanatos.

## The Arts and Crafts Movement
- William Morris (England, 1860s–1890s): the idea that industrial mass production was destroying beauty and craftsmanship.
- Morris wanted to reunite art and craft — handmade furniture, textiles, wallpaper, books.
- Art Nouveau inherited this idea but embraced the machine age rather than rejecting it. Mucha's posters were mass-produced. Art Nouveau was democratic — it wanted beauty in everyday life, not just in museums.

## Art Nouveau Across Europe
The same impulse, different names:
- **Art Nouveau** — France, Belgium
- **Jugendstil** — Germany, Austria
- **Stile Liberty** — Italy
- **Modernisme** — Catalonia (Gaudí)
- **Glasgow Style** — Scotland (Mackintosh)

## The Backlash
- By 1910, Art Nouveau was already being attacked as excessive, decadent, and superficial.
- Adolf Loos's **"Ornament and Crime"** (1908/1913) — argued that ornament was a waste of labor and material, a sign of cultural degeneracy. "The evolution of culture is synonymous with the removal of ornament from objects of daily use."
- This essay basically predicted Modernism — the clean lines, the rejection of decoration, the "less is more" philosophy that would dominate the 20th century.
- Louis Sullivan's **"Form and Function"** — the American parallel. "Form ever follows function." The idea that a building's appearance should express its purpose, not disguise it.

## The Big Question
Should art serve the soul or serve daily life? Can it do both?

Symbolism says: art is about the inner world — dreams, myths, the unconscious. It serves the soul.
Art Nouveau says: art should be everywhere — in your building, your furniture, your poster, your jewelry. It serves daily life.

Klimt does both. That's why he's the central figure.

# Primary Texts — Episode 6

## Adolf Loos — "Ornament and Crime" (1908/1913)
> "The evolution of culture is synonymous with the removal of ornament from objects of daily use."

> "Ornament is wasted labor power and hence wasted health. It has always been so. But today it also means wasted material, and both together mean wasted capital."

> "Freedom from ornament is a sign of spiritual strength."

### For the Script
Loos is the counterargument to everything Art Nouveau stands for. He's the voice saying: all this beauty is actually a disease. Whether you agree or not, his essay predicted the entire 20th century of design.

## Louis Sullivan — "The Tall Office Building Artistically Considered" (1896)
> "It is the pervading law of all things organic and inorganic, of all things physical and metaphysical, of all things human and all things superhuman, of all true manifestations of the head, of the heart, of the soul, that the life is recognizable in its expression, that form ever follows function. This is the law."

### For the Script
Sullivan is the American version of the same argument. "Form follows function" — four words that shaped modern architecture.

## Vienna Secession Motto
> "Der Zeit ihre Kunst, der Kunst ihre Freiheit."
> "To every age its art, to every art its freedom."

Inscribed on the Secession Building. Put it on screen.
