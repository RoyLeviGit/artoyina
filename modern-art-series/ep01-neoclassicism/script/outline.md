# Episode 1 Outline: "The Man Who Wanted to Be Greek"
## Movement: Neo-Classicism | Runtime Target: 35–45 min

---

### 1. COLD OPEN (2–3 min)
**Scene:** Paris, January 21, 1793. A painter sits in the National Convention. A vote is being taken. The question: should the King of France be executed? The painter raises his hand. Yes. He votes yes.

That painter — Jacques-Louis David — was the most famous artist in France. And he had just helped kill a king. But to understand how a *painter* ended up voting on life and death, we need to go back. Way back. To a German librarian who never went to Greece.

---

### 2. "OKAY, LET ME BACK UP" — The World Before David (5–8 min)

**The Rococo problem:**
- Show Fragonard's *The Swing*. Describe it. It's gorgeous and it's fluff.
- The aristocracy loved this stuff. Critics were starting to hate it.
- Diderot's critique: Boucher "has everything — except truth."

**The Greece obsession:**
- Pompeii and Herculaneum excavated. Ancient world suddenly visible.
- Enter Winckelmann. The librarian's son who invented art history.
- "Noble simplicity and quiet grandeur" — put it on screen.
- The irony: he never went to Greece. His Greece was imaginary.
- His murder in Trieste (brief — don't dwell, but it's a dramatic beat).

**The system:**
- The Salon: the only game in town.
- The hierarchy of genres. History painting at the top.
- The Prix de Rome: win it or die trying. David failed three times.

---

### 3. THE MAIN CHARACTER — David's Rise (10–12 min)

**The failures and the breakthrough:**
- Three Prix de Rome failures. The reported suicide attempt by starvation.
- Fourth time: he wins. Goes to Rome. Everything changes.

**Rome:**
- Winckelmann's ideas hit him in person. Ancient sculpture, Raphael, Caravaggio.
- Comes back to Paris a different man.

**The paintings that made him:**
- *Oath of the Horatii* (1784) — THE painting. Spend time here.
  - Describe it in detail. The geometry. The swords. The weeping women.
  - People wept in front of it. It was a cultural event.
  - The message: duty to the state above everything. Even family.
- *Death of Socrates* (1787) — Principle over survival.
- *Brutus* (1789) — A father who executed his own sons for treason. Displayed weeks after the Bastille fell. The authorities tried to remove it. The crowd said no.

**The turn:** These aren't just paintings anymore. They're political statements. And the Revolution is about to make that literal.

---

### 4. THE REVOLUTION — David in Power (8–10 min)

**David becomes a revolutionary:**
- Elected to the National Convention. Sits with the Jacobins.
- Votes for the king's death.
- Becomes the Revolution's art director: festivals, uniforms, imagery.
- His friendship with Robespierre. "If you drink hemlock, I will drink it with you."

**The Death of Marat (1793):**
- This is the centerpiece. Spend real time here.
- The story: Marat murdered in his bath by Charlotte Corday.
- David paints it immediately. It's a secular Pietà.
- Walk through the painting: the simplicity, the letter in Marat's hand, the knife on the floor, the wooden block inscribed "À MARAT, DAVID."
- It's propaganda. It's also one of the greatest paintings ever made. Sit with that contradiction. Don't resolve it.

**The dark side:**
- David signed arrest warrants. People died.
- This isn't abstract. The man who painted virtue was complicit in state violence.

---

### 5. THE FALL AND THE PIVOT (5–7 min)

**Robespierre falls:**
- July 27, 1794. David arrested the next day.
- Prison. The self-portrait — maybe the most honest thing he ever painted.
- "I was deceived by the mask of patriotism." Was he? Or was he just saving his skin?

**Napoleon:**
- David meets Napoleon. Sees a new hero.
- *Napoleon Crossing the Alps* — the iconic image. Napoleon on a rearing horse. In reality: a mule. But the painting became the truth.
- *The Coronation* — 30 feet wide. David rearranges reality (Napoleon's mother).
- The Canova connection: *Napoleon as Mars*. Napoleon hated it. Too idealized. The man who loved David's propaganda rejected Canova's idealization. Why? Because David made him look powerful. Canova made him look like a Greek god. Napoleon wanted power, not beauty.

**Exile and death:**
- Bourbons return. David exiled as a regicide.
- Dies in Brussels, 1825. France won't take his body back.
- He's still there.

---

### 6. THE LEGACY — Ingres and the Bridge (3–5 min)

**Ingres as heir:**
- David's best student. Carries the torch.
- But Ingres is weird — the *Grande Odalisque* with the extra vertebrae. The champion of rules who keeps breaking them.
- He'll become one half of the great rivalry of the 19th century.

**The big question (direct to camera):**
- David believed art should serve morality and the state. His paintings are genuinely beautiful. They're also weapons. He voted to kill a king. He signed death warrants. And he made some of the most powerful images in Western art.
- Can art be both beautiful and propaganda? And if it can — should it be?

**Closing hook:**
> "David built a world where art served reason, duty, the state. His student Ingres would defend that world for decades. But not everyone agreed. One painter was about to blow the whole thing up — with color, chaos, and a trip to North Africa that changed everything. His name was Eugène Delacroix."
