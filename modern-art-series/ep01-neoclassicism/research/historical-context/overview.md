# Historical Context: Neo-Classicism (~1760–1815)

## What Was Happening in the World

### The Enlightenment
- The intellectual backdrop to everything. Reason, logic, science, individual rights — the idea that humanity could *think* its way to a better world.
- Key figures: Voltaire, Rousseau, Diderot, Kant. These aren't art people, but their ideas saturated the culture.
- The Encyclopédie (1751–1772) — Diderot's attempt to catalog all human knowledge. The Enlightenment in book form.
- The core belief: the ancient world (especially Greece and Rome) had figured out how to build a rational, virtuous society. If we could just get back to those principles...

### The Excavations
- **Herculaneum** rediscovered in 1738. **Pompeii** in 1748.
- Suddenly, ancient Roman daily life was visible — not just ruins and statues, but houses, frescoes, furniture, graffiti.
- This triggered a massive wave of interest in antiquity across Europe. It wasn't just academic — it was fashionable. Everyone wanted Greek and Roman everything: furniture, architecture, clothing, hairstyles.
- The Grand Tour became essential for any wealthy young European — travel to Rome, see the ruins, come back "cultured."

### The French Revolution (1789–1799)
- The political earthquake that Neo-Classicism got swept up in.
- Key dates for the episode:
  - 1789: Storming of the Bastille (July 14)
  - 1792: Monarchy abolished, Republic declared
  - 1793: Louis XVI executed (January 21) — David voted for this
  - 1793: Reign of Terror begins. Marat assassinated (July 13) — David paints him
  - 1794: Robespierre falls (July 27) — David arrested the next day
- The Revolution adopted Neo-Classical imagery wholesale. Roman Republic = French Republic. Senators in togas. Fasces as symbols. The whole aesthetic was: we are the new Rome.

### Napoleon (1799–1815)
- Seized power in 1799 (the coup of 18 Brumaire).
- Crowned himself Emperor in 1804.
- Used art as propaganda more deliberately than any leader since Augustus. David was his primary tool.
- Napoleon's campaigns spread Neo-Classical style across Europe — it became the style of empire.
- Waterloo (1815) and exile to Saint Helena. The Bourbon monarchy restored. David exiled.

## The Art World Before Neo-Classicism

### The Rococo (what Neo-Classicism was reacting against)
- Light, playful, decorative, aristocratic. Think Fragonard's *The Swing* — a woman in a fluffy dress on a swing, her shoe flying off, a man peeking up her skirt. It's charming, sexy, and completely frivolous.
- Boucher, Watteau, Fragonard — painters of the aristocracy, for the aristocracy.
- By the 1760s, critics were calling Rococo decadent, immoral, symptomatic of a corrupt ruling class. Sound familiar? The same critique that would fuel the Revolution.

### The Salon
- The official art exhibition of the Académie Royale de Peinture et de Sculpture.
- Held every year (later every two years) at the Louvre.
- **This was the only way to build a career as an artist.** No galleries, no dealers, no Instagram. The Salon jury decided who was seen and who wasn't.
- Hierarchy of genres (top to bottom):
  1. History painting (mythological, biblical, classical scenes) — the "highest" form
  2. Portraiture
  3. Genre painting (scenes of daily life)
  4. Landscape
  5. Still life
- This hierarchy matters because it explains why David painted Greek heroes and not, say, fruit bowls. And it explains why Courbet painting a funeral in Episode 3 was so radical.

### The Prix de Rome
- The most prestigious prize for young artists. Winners got a funded residency at the French Academy in Rome.
- David failed three times before winning. The system was brutal and political.
- Winning the Prix de Rome essentially guaranteed your career. Losing could end it.

## The Big Question for the Episode
Neo-Classicism wasn't just an aesthetic choice — it was a moral and political argument. The claim was: art should make you a better citizen. It should teach virtue, sacrifice, duty. It should serve the state.

The question to pose to the viewer: **Can art be both beautiful AND political propaganda? And if it can — should it be?**

David's career is the perfect case study because he lived both sides. His paintings are genuinely great art. They're also tools of political power that were used to justify violence. Both things are true at the same time.
