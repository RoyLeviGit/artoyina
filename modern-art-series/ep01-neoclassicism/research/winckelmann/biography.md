# Johann Joachim Winckelmann (1717–1768)

## Why He Matters
He never painted a single painting, but he's arguably the person most responsible for Neo-Classicism existing. He was an art historian — really the *first* art historian in the modern sense — who convinced an entire continent that ancient Greece was the pinnacle of human civilization and that all art should aspire to imitate it.

## Life Story

- Born in Stendal, Prussia, 1717. Son of a cobbler. Dirt poor.
- Brilliant student. Taught himself Greek and Latin. Worked as a schoolteacher and librarian to survive.
- In 1755, published **"Thoughts on the Imitation of Greek Works in Painting and Sculpture"** — the text that started everything. His famous phrase: Greek art embodies **"noble simplicity and quiet grandeur"** (*edle Einfalt und stille Größe*). This became the motto of an entire movement.
- Converted from Protestantism to Catholicism (likely for career reasons — it gave him access to Rome).
- Moved to Rome in 1755. Became librarian to Cardinal Albani. Had access to the greatest collections of ancient art in the world.
- Published **"History of the Art of Antiquity"** (1764) — the first systematic history of art organized by style and period rather than by artist biography. This book invented art history as a discipline.
- Here's the irony: **Winckelmann never went to Greece.** His idea of Greek art was based almost entirely on Roman copies. He was building a fantasy of Greece based on secondhand evidence.
- Murdered in Trieste in 1768 by a man he'd met at an inn. He was 50. The circumstances suggest a sexual encounter gone wrong — Winckelmann was almost certainly gay, which adds another layer to his idealization of the male Greek body.

## Key Ideas

1. **Greek art = the universal ideal.** Not just one style among many, but the *best* art humans ever made. Everything since has been decline.
2. **"Noble simplicity and quiet grandeur"** — Great art should be calm, restrained, balanced. No excessive emotion. No drama. Think of the Apollo Belvedere: perfect proportions, serene expression, idealized body.
3. **Art reflects the society that makes it.** Greek art was great because Greek society was free. This was a political argument disguised as an aesthetic one.
4. **Imitation, not copying.** Artists shouldn't copy Greek statues — they should internalize the *principles* and apply them to new subjects.

## What to Emphasize in the Episode
- He's the origin point. Without Winckelmann, David doesn't become David.
- The irony of never visiting Greece is gold for the script — he invented an entire aesthetic philosophy based on an imaginary Greece.
- His murder is a dramatic detail but handle it with care — don't sensationalize his sexuality, but don't erase it either. It's part of understanding the man.
- The phrase "noble simplicity and quiet grandeur" should probably appear on screen — it's the DNA of the whole movement.

## Quotes
- "The only way for us to become great, or even inimitable if possible, is to imitate the Greeks."

- "As the depth of the sea always remains calm however much the surface may be agitated, so does the expression in the figures of the Greeks reveal a great and composed soul in the midst of passions."
