# Primary Texts & Key Passages — Episode 1: Neo-Classicism

---

## 1. Winckelmann — "Thoughts on the Imitation of Greek Works in Painting and Sculpture" (1755)

### The Key Passage
> "The only way for us to become great, or even inimitable if possible, is to imitate the Greeks. [...] Their masterpieces reveal not only nature at its most beautiful but also something beyond nature, namely certain ideal forms of its beauty, which, as an ancient interpreter of Plato teaches us, come from images created by the mind alone."

### "Noble Simplicity and Quiet Grandeur"
> "The general and most distinctive characteristics of the Greek masterpieces are, finally, a noble simplicity and quiet grandeur, both in posture and expression. Just as the depths of the sea always remain calm however much the surface may rage, so does the expression in the figures of the Greeks reveal a great and composed soul in the midst of passions."

### Why This Matters for the Script
This is the founding text. Everything flows from here. The phrase "noble simplicity and quiet grandeur" should probably be on screen. It's six words that defined a century of art.

**Source:** Full text available on various academic sites. Originally published in German as *Gedanken über die Nachahmung der griechischen Werke in der Malerei und Bildhauerkunst*.

## 2. Winckelmann — "History of the Art of Antiquity" (1764)

### Key Idea
Art develops in stages that mirror the rise and fall of civilizations. Greek art peaked during the age of Pericles (5th century BC), then declined. All subsequent art is measured against that peak.

### Useful Passage
> "Art, which has a similar relationship to the culture of a people as a great river has to the tributaries that feed it, reached its highest point among the Greeks."

### Why This Matters
This book invented art history as a discipline. Before Winckelmann, people wrote about individual artists (Vasari's *Lives*). Winckelmann wrote about *styles* and *periods*. That's the framework we still use today.

## 3. David — Statements and Letters

### On the Purpose of Art
> "In the arts, the way in which an idea is rendered, and the manner in which it is expressed, is much more important than the idea itself."

### On Napoleon (after their first meeting, 1797)
> "Oh my friends, what a beautiful head he has! It is pure, it is great, it is as beautiful as the antique! [...] Bonaparte is my hero."

### To Robespierre
> "If you drink hemlock, I will drink it with you."
(This quote is widely attributed but its exact source is debated. Use it, but you could note the uncertainty if you want to be rigorous.)

### On the Oath of the Horatii
David reportedly said he wanted to work "in a pure Greek style" — though the painting's setting is Roman, not Greek. The distinction didn't matter much to him; "Greek" meant "ancient and virtuous."

### From Prison (1795)
> "I was deceived by the mask of patriotism."
(After Robespierre's fall, David tried to distance himself. Whether this was genuine remorse or self-preservation is one of the great questions about him.)

## 4. Denis Diderot — Salon Reviews

### Why Include Diderot
He wasn't a Neo-Classicist, but his Salon reviews (written 1759–1781) are the best contemporary criticism of the period. He championed moral seriousness in art before David arrived.

### Key Passage (on Greuze, a proto-Neo-Classical painter)
> "Here is your painter and mine — the first among us who thought of giving art morals and manners, and of linking events together so that it would be easy to make a novel out of them."

### On Boucher (the Rococo painter he despised)
> "What colors! What variety! What a wealth of objects and ideas! This man has everything — except truth."

### Why This Matters
Diderot articulates the *demand* that David would supply. The culture was already hungry for moral seriousness in art. David didn't create that hunger — he fed it.

## 5. Suggested Further Reading (for your own prep, not necessarily for the script)

- Hugh Honour, *Neo-Classicism* (1968) — still the standard overview
- Simon Lee, *David* (1999) — best single-volume biography
- Alex Potts, *Flesh and the Ideal: Winckelmann and the Origins of Art History* (1994) — deep dive on Winckelmann
- T.J. Clark, *Farewell to an Idea* (1999) — Chapter 1 on David and the Revolution is brilliant
