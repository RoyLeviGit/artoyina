# Jean-Auguste-Dominique Ingres (1780–1867)

## Role in the Episode
David's most important student and the man who carried Neo-Classicism into the next generation. But he's also the bridge to Episode 2 — his rivalry with Delacroix is one of the great feuds in art history.

## Key Facts
- Born in Montauban, southern France, 1780. His father was a minor artist.
- Entered David's studio at age 17. Quickly became the star pupil.
- Won the Prix de Rome in 1801 but couldn't actually go to Rome until 1806 due to funding issues.
- Lived in Italy for 18 years (1806–1824). This long exile shaped him — he became more obsessed with Raphael than with ancient sculpture.
- Returned to Paris and became the undisputed champion of the "line" camp — drawing, contour, precision, idealized form.

## The Ingres Paradox
Here's what makes him interesting: he preached classical purity, but his actual paintings are *weird*. 
- **Grande Odalisque** (1814) — A reclining nude with an impossibly long back. Critics counted: she has two or three extra vertebrae. Ingres didn't care. He distorted anatomy for beauty. This is supposedly the guy defending "correct" drawing.
- His portraits are extraordinary — psychologically sharp, obsessively detailed in fabrics and textures, almost photographic. They're his best work, but he considered them beneath him.
- He wanted to be a history painter. He was actually a portraitist. The gap between ambition and talent is very human.

## The Rivalry with Delacroix
- Ingres = line, drawing, intellect, tradition, the Académie
- Delacroix = color, paint, emotion, rebellion, the Romantics
- They reportedly couldn't stand each other. At one Salon, Ingres allegedly demanded that windows be opened after Delacroix's paintings were hung, saying the room smelled of sulfur.
- This wasn't just personal — it was a philosophical war about what art *is*.

## Key Works for the Episode
- *Grande Odalisque* (1814) — the "perfect" classicist who distorts the body
- *The Apotheosis of Homer* (1827) — his statement painting: Homer enthroned, surrounded by the great artists of history. It's a manifesto for tradition.
- *The Turkish Bath* (1862) — painted when he was 82. A circular canvas packed with nude women. Sensual, voyeuristic, and deeply strange for a man who preached restraint.

## What to Emphasize
- He's the heir, not the hero. David is the main character; Ingres is the legacy.
- The paradox: the champion of rules who kept breaking them
- Set up the Delacroix rivalry as the closing hook into Episode 2
