# Antonio Canova (1757–1822)

## Role in the Episode
The sculptor who proves Neo-Classicism wasn't just a painting movement. Canova is to sculpture what David is to painting — the definitive Neo-Classical artist. He also connects to Napoleon (he sculpted him) and to the broader European scope of the movement.

## Key Facts
- Born in Possagno, near Venice, 1757. Orphaned young, raised by his grandfather, a stonemason.
- Showed talent early — carving butter sculptures for banquets as a child (great anecdote).
- Moved to Rome in 1781. Quickly became the most famous sculptor in Europe.
- Worked for popes, emperors, and kings. Everyone wanted a Canova.
- After Napoleon's fall, Canova was sent to Paris to negotiate the return of Italian artworks that Napoleon had looted. He succeeded — a diplomat as well as an artist.
- Died in Venice, 1822.

## Key Works
- **Psyche Revived by Cupid's Kiss** (1787–1793) — Two figures in an impossible, gravity-defying embrace. It looks like marble shouldn't be able to do this. The surface is so polished it almost looks like skin. This is the piece to show if you want people to understand what Neo-Classical sculpture could achieve.
- **Napoleon as Mars the Peacemaker** (1802–1806) — A colossal nude statue of Napoleon as a Greek god. Napoleon hated it. He thought it was too athletic, too idealized — he didn't look like that and he knew it. He had it hidden away. The irony: the man who used David's paintings as propaganda rejected Canova's sculpture for being *too* idealized. There's a great tension there.
- **Paolina Borghese as Venus Victrix** (1805–1808) — Napoleon's sister, reclining as Venus. When asked if she was uncomfortable posing nearly nude, she reportedly said: "No, the studio was heated." Incredible line.

## What to Emphasize
- The Napoleon statue rejection is a perfect story beat — it shows the limits of idealization. Even Napoleon thought it was too much.
- Canova's marble technique was genuinely revolutionary — he achieved a surface finish that made stone look like flesh. Worth a visual moment: zoom into the marble texture.
- He represents the international reach of Neo-Classicism — this wasn't just a French thing. Italian sculptor, working for French emperors, inspired by German theory (Winckelmann).
- Keep it brief — he's a supporting character, not the lead. One or two minutes max.
