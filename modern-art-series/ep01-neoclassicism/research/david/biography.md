# Jacques-Louis David (1748–1825)

## The Short Version
The most politically entangled painter in history. He didn't just paint the Revolution — he *was* the Revolution. Then he switched sides and became Napoleon's image-maker. He's the reason we think of Napoleon the way we do.

## Life Timeline

### Early Years (1748–1774)
- Born in Paris, August 30, 1748. His father was killed in a duel when David was nine.
- Raised by well-off uncles who wanted him to be an architect. He insisted on painting.
- Studied under Joseph-Marie Vien, a proto-Neoclassical painter.
- Failed the Prix de Rome **three times** (1770, 1771, 1772). The third failure devastated him — he reportedly attempted to starve himself to death.
- Finally won the Prix de Rome in 1774 on his fourth attempt.

### Rome & Transformation (1775–1780)
- Arrived in Rome and was initially unimpressed. Then Winckelmann's ideas hit him.
- Spent five years studying ancient sculpture, Raphael, and Caravaggio.
- Came back to Paris a completely different painter — austere, morally serious, obsessed with ancient virtue.

### Rise to Fame (1781–1789)
- *Belisarius Begging for Alms* (1781) — his first major Salon success. A Roman general reduced to begging. The message: even the greatest can be destroyed by an ungrateful state.
- **Oath of the Horatii** (1784) — THE painting. Three brothers swearing to fight to the death for Rome. Rigid geometry, masculine duty, sacrifice for the state. It was a sensation. People literally wept in front of it. This is the painting that launched Neo-Classicism as a full-blown movement.
- *The Death of Socrates* (1787) — Socrates calmly taking the hemlock while his students lose it. The message: principle over survival.
- *The Lictors Bring to Brutus the Bodies of His Sons* (1789) — Brutus has ordered the execution of his own sons for treason against Rome. Displayed just weeks after the storming of the Bastille. The authorities tried to pull it from the Salon. The crowd demanded it stay.

### The Revolution (1789–1794)
- This is where it gets wild. David didn't just support the Revolution — he became one of its key figures.
- Elected to the National Convention in 1792. Sat with the radical Montagnards (the Jacobins).
- **Voted for the execution of Louis XVI.** Yes, a painter voted to kill the king.
- Became essentially the Revolution's art director — designed festivals, uniforms, the new Republican calendar's imagery.
- Close ally of Robespierre. Reportedly said to him: "If you drink hemlock, I will drink it with you."
- **The Death of Marat** (1793) — His masterpiece. Marat murdered in his bathtub by Charlotte Corday. David painted it as a secular Pietà — Marat as a revolutionary Christ. It's propaganda, and it's one of the greatest paintings ever made. The tension between those two facts is the whole point.
- Signed arrest warrants. People he signed warrants for were executed. This is not abstract — David had blood on his hands.

### Fall & Imprisonment (1794–1795)
- Robespierre fell on 9 Thermidor (July 27, 1794). David was arrested the next day.
- Imprisoned twice. Narrowly avoided the guillotine himself.
- In prison, he painted a self-portrait — looking at himself honestly for maybe the first time. It's a remarkable image.
- His students lobbied for his release. He was freed in late 1795.

### Napoleon (1797–1815)
- Met Napoleon in 1797. Immediately saw a new hero to paint.
- **Napoleon Crossing the Alps** (1801) — The iconic image. Napoleon asked to be painted "calm on a fiery horse." David obliged. It's pure mythology — Napoleon actually crossed the Alps on a mule. But the painting *became* the truth.
- **The Coronation of Napoleon** (1805–1807) — Massive canvas (over 30 feet wide). Napoleon crowning Josephine at Notre-Dame. David was there in person, sketching. He rearranged reality: Napoleon's mother is prominently placed in the painting, but she actually boycotted the ceremony.
- Became First Painter to the Emperor. Received the Légion d'honneur.

### Exile & Death (1816–1825)
- After Waterloo and the Bourbon Restoration, David was exiled as a regicide (he'd voted to kill Louis XVI — they didn't forget).
- Moved to Brussels. Continued painting but his work became softer, less urgent.
- Died in Brussels on December 29, 1825. France refused to allow his body to return. He's still buried in Brussels.

## Key Themes for the Episode
1. **Art as political weapon** — David proves that a painting can be as powerful as a speech or a law
2. **The true believer problem** — He believed in the Revolution, then Napoleon, with equal fervor. Was he principled or just drawn to power?
3. **The gap between the art and the man** — His paintings preach virtue, sacrifice, moral clarity. The man himself was politically opportunistic and complicit in state violence.
4. **The prison self-portrait** — Maybe the most honest thing he ever made, precisely because it wasn't for anyone else

## Quotes to Use
- "In the arts, the way in which an idea is rendered, and the manner in which it is expressed, is much more important than the idea itself."
- To Robespierre: "If you drink hemlock, I will drink it with you."
- On the Oath of the Horatii: "I want to work in a pure Greek style."
- After seeing Napoleon: "Oh my friends, what a beautiful head he has! It is pure, it is great, it is as beautiful as the antique!"
