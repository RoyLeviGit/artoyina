## 2. Factual / verification flags

These are things that might be wrong or that the script presents as settled when they're not — same category as the "father killed in a duel when he was nine" issue from Chapter 1.

- **"Delacroix posed for one of the figures on the Raft"** — commonly repeated but disputed. Some sources say he modeled for the figure seen from behind; others question the attribution. Hedge it: "who may even have posed for one of the figures on it — accounts vary."

- **"Victor Hugo would later use this figure [the boy with pistols] as inspiration for Gavroche"** — this is a very common claim but its sourcing is weak. Hugo never explicitly said this. Worth adding "reportedly" or cutting if you can't verify it.

- **"He reportedly went back to his own painting, days before the exhibition opened, and repainted parts of it"** (Constable influence on Massacre at Chios) — the story is widely cited in art history and generally accepted, but "days before" is a very specific claim. Worth verifying the timeframe.

- **"His studio contained over nine thousand works"** — this is a very large number. The commonly cited figure for Delacroix's estate is around 9,000 items total (including sketches, notebooks, studies), but calling all of it "works" inflates the impression. Consider: "His studio and notebooks contained thousands of works — studies, sketches, finished paintings."

- **"His funeral was attended by Monet, Renoir, Bazille"** — this is frequently cited but the specific attendees vary by source. Bazille is well-documented; Monet and Renoir are less certain. Either hedge it ("several of the young painters who would become the Impressionists") or verify each name before using them.

- **Géricault died from horse-riding accidents** — the script says "a series of horse-riding accidents had left him bedridden." The horse-riding injuries are real and well-documented, but his death had multiple contributing causes (tuberculosis, general physical decline). The framing as purely riding-related is a simplification. Consider: "Years of horse-riding injuries had left him bedridden, his health in collapse."

- **"roughly sixteen by twenty-three feet"** for the Raft — the actual dimensions are approximately 491 × 716 cm (16 × 23.5 ft). This is close. Add the metric equivalent: *[ON-SCREEN TEXT: approx. 4.9 × 7.2 m / 16 × 23.5 ft]*

## 5. Voice / humor notes

- **Friedrich's backstory** — "He'd lost his mother at seven, a sister at eight, and his brother drowned saving him from falling through ice when he was thirteen." This is delivered very flatly in the script. It's actually a devastating accumulation — you could give it a beat: something like *[slight pause]* "There's a lot going on in that biography." Or lean into the dark comedy of it. Your call, but it lands harder with some kind of acknowledgment.

- **"One critic called it 'the massacre of painting.' Delacroix took that as a compliment."** — this is a good line. Make sure to land it with a pause before moving on. It earns a moment.

- **Ingres / Delacroix rivalry details** — the windows-and-sulfur story and the committee scene are good, but they read as anecdotes rattled off at pace. Both deserve their own breath. Consider slowing down: present one, let it land, then the other. Right now they're stacked.

- **"Ingres reportedly said that Delacroix painted with a drunken broom"** — "reportedly" is doing a lot of work. Worth noting in production notes whether this quote is well-sourced or apocryphal. It appears widely but the original source is unclear.

- **The Winckelmann/Morocco irony paragraph** ("He went to North Africa and what he saw was... ancient Greece") — this is already the most satisfying conceptual payoff in the script. It could afford a joke. Something like: "Winckelmann invented an imaginary Greece from Roman copies. David painted it from sculptures. And Delacroix found it in Morocco. If Winckelmann had been alive to hear this, he probably would have been delighted, and also deeply confused."

## 6. Art technique — missing visual analysis

Same note as Chapter 1: the script tells you what paintings *mean* but not much about how they *work*. This is most noticeable in:

- **The Raft of the Medusa** — the detail breakdown is strong on narrative, but there's nothing about the painting's technique. The palette is dark and theatrical (inspired by Caravaggio — Géricault studied him in Rome). The surface is impasted in places, almost sculptural. The composition deliberately echoes classical structures (a triangle, like the Horatii) while filling it with chaos. Even one sentence on this would add dimension.

- **Sardanapalus** — good comparison to David's rigidity, but could add: the color temperature (hot reds and golds against cool shadow), the fact that Delacroix used *color* itself to create movement rather than line. This is what Ingres found so threatening — the brushwork was doing work that, in academic painting, only drawing was supposed to do.

## 8. The colonial critique section (Act 5)

"I'm not going to resolve this. I don't think it can be resolved. But it needs to be named." — this is thoughtful, but it's also slightly evasive in a way that could read as defensive. You might want to think about whether you want to *say* something here — even briefly — rather than just naming the issue and moving on. No prescription, just a flag that "naming it and moving on" has its own implicit stance.

Also: "a European man, traveling under colonial protection, projecting his own fantasies onto a culture that was being colonized by his country" — this is accurate framing, but it reads a bit like a lecture compared to the rest of the script's tone. Consider whether you can say it in your own voice rather than in the vocabulary of a critical studies paper. What do *you* actually think when you look at Women of Algiers knowing this context?
