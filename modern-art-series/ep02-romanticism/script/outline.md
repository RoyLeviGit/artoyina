# Episode 2 Outline: "The Painter Who Chose Chaos"
## Movement: Romanticism | Runtime Target: 35–45 min

---

### 1. COLD OPEN (2–3 min)
**Scene:** A studio in Paris, 1818. A young painter has shaved his head. The room smells like death — literally. There are severed limbs from the morgue on the floor. He's built a wooden raft in the middle of the room. He's been interviewing survivors of a shipwreck — a shipwreck that involved cannibalism. And he's going to turn it into the largest painting anyone has ever seen.

His name was Théodore Géricault. He was 27 years old. And the painting he was about to make would crack open the door to a completely new kind of art.

---

### 2. "OKAY, LET ME BACK UP" — The World After David (5–8 min)

**Where we left off:**
- David is in exile. Ingres is holding the fort. The Académie says: line, reason, classical subjects.
- But Europe has changed. Napoleon is gone. The old monarchies are back. And people are angry, restless, disillusioned.

**The Romantic turn:**
- The Enlightenment promised reason would fix everything. It didn't. The Revolution ate its own. Napoleon conquered and fell.
- A new generation asks: what if reason isn't enough? What if the most important truths are the ones you *feel*?
- In literature: Byron, Shelley, Hugo. In music: Beethoven (his late work). In philosophy: the rehabilitation of emotion, intuition, the irrational.

**The sublime:**
- Explain it simply. "That feeling at the edge of a cliff — terrified and amazed at the same time."
- Show Friedrich's *Wanderer Above the Sea of Fog*. Hold on it. That's the sublime in one image.
- The Romantics didn't want beauty. They wanted *overwhelm*.

---

### 3. THE RAFT — Géricault's Masterpiece (8–10 min)

**The real story of the Medusa:**
- 1816. A French frigate runs aground because the captain is an incompetent political appointee.
- 147 people on a raft. Tow ropes cut. 13 days adrift. Madness, murder, cannibalism. 15 survivors.
- Two survivors publish a book. It becomes a scandal — an indictment of the restored monarchy.

**Géricault's obsession:**
- Reads the book. Interviews the survivors. Builds a raft. Visits the morgue. Shaves his head.
- 18 months of work. The painting is 16 × 23 feet.
- Walk through the painting: the pyramid of bodies, the dead at the bottom, the desperate at the top, the tiny ship on the horizon. Hope and horror in the same frame.

**The aftermath:**
- Mixed reception at the Salon. The government hated it (it was about their failure).
- Géricault takes it to London — sensation.
- He dies at 32. Horse-riding accidents. His friend Delacroix inherits the Romantic mantle.

**The pivot:** "Géricault proved that a painting could be about *now* — about real suffering, real scandal, real bodies. But his friend Eugène Delacroix was about to take that energy and turn it into something even more radical."

---

### 4. THE MAIN CHARACTER — Delacroix (10–12 min)

**The mystery of his birth:**
- Officially the son of a diplomat. Probably the son of Talleyrand. He looked like Talleyrand. Talleyrand protected his career. Neither confirmed it.

**Early career:**
- *The Barque of Dante* (1822) — Salon debut at 24. Dark, violent, visceral.
- *Massacre at Chios* (1824) — A real atrocity, painted with raw emotion. "The massacre of painting."
- Sees Constable's landscapes and repaints parts of his own work days before the exhibition. He's absorbing everything.

**Sardanapalus (1827):**
- Show it. An Assyrian king watching everything burn. Chaos, violence, sensuality, all at once.
- This is the anti-David painting. Where David's compositions are rigid and geometric, this is a whirlpool.
- The Salon was horrified. Even his supporters were uneasy.

**The rivalry with Ingres:**
- Line vs. color. Mind vs. gut. Tradition vs. rebellion.
- The sulfur anecdote. The "drunken broom" insult. The "incomplete intelligence" comeback.
- This wasn't petty — it was a philosophical war about what art is *for*.

**Liberty Leading the People (1830):**
- The July Revolution. Three days of street fighting.
- Delacroix wasn't a revolutionary. "Since I have not fought for the glory of my country, at least I will paint for her."
- Walk through the painting: Marianne, the tricolor, the boy with pistols, the dead underfoot.
- Compare to David's political paintings: David was a true believer. Delacroix painted the *feeling* of revolution, not its ideology. That's the difference between Neo-Classicism and Romanticism in one comparison.

---

### 5. NORTH AFRICA — The Trip That Changed Everything (5–7 min)

**The journey (1832):**
- Diplomatic mission to Morocco and Algeria. France had just invaded Algeria.
- Delacroix fills notebooks. He's overwhelmed.
- "The Greeks and Romans are here at my door." — He goes to Africa and sees ancient Greece in living people. The irony is extraordinary.

**Women of Algiers (1834):**
- Painted from memory after visiting a harem.
- Intimate, sensual, richly detailed. A window into a world European viewers had never seen.
- Picasso later painted 15 variations of this single painting.

**The Orientalism question:**
- Delacroix was genuinely fascinated. He was also a European man projecting his fantasies onto a colonized culture.
- Don't resolve this tension — name it. It connects directly to Gauguin in Episode 5.

---

### 6. BAUDELAIRE & THE LEGACY (3–5 min)

**The poet who explained the painters:**
- Baudelaire's definition: "Romanticism is precisely situated neither in choice of subjects nor in exact truth, but in a mode of feeling." Put it on screen.
- He championed Delacroix for decades. Called him "the most suggestive of all painters."

**Delacroix's late years:**
- Major mural commissions. His late work gets looser, almost abstract.
- Dies in 1863. His funeral is attended by the young painters who will become the Impressionists.

**The big question (direct to camera):**
- Romanticism said: feel everything. The mind isn't enough. Art should overwhelm you.
- But there's a risk: if you chase feeling, where do you stop? Sardanapalus is magnificent and it's also kind of insane.

**Closing hook:**
> "Romanticism said: feel everything. But what if you just... looked? What if instead of painting gods and heroes and revolutions, you painted the world exactly as it was — ugly parts and all? What if you painted a funeral in a small town, with ugly people and a dog, at the size of a history painting? One man did exactly that. And everyone in Paris lost their mind."
