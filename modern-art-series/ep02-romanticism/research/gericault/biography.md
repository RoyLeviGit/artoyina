# Théodore Géricault (1791–1824)

## Why He Matters
He died at 32 and left behind one painting that changed everything. *The Raft of the Medusa* is one of the most insane stories in art history — both the event it depicts and the process of making it.

## Life (Brief)
- Born in Rouen, 1791. Wealthy family.
- Studied under Guérin (same studio where he met Delacroix).
- Passionate about horses — many of his early works are equestrian scenes.
- Had a scandalous affair with his uncle's young wife. She became pregnant. The affair was discovered. Géricault fled to Italy partly to escape the fallout.
- Returned to Paris and became obsessed with a shipwreck.
- Died in 1824 after a series of horse-riding accidents left him bedridden. He was 32.

## The Raft of the Medusa (1819) — The Full Story

### The Event
- June 1816: The French frigate *Méduse* ran aground off the coast of Senegal due to the incompetence of its captain (a political appointee with almost no sailing experience — he got the job through connections to the restored Bourbon monarchy).
- There weren't enough lifeboats. 147 people were put on a hastily built raft and towed behind the lifeboats.
- The tow ropes were cut. Whether deliberately or accidentally is still debated. The raft was set adrift.
- **13 days on the open ocean.** What followed: starvation, dehydration, madness, murder, and cannibalism. When the raft was finally spotted by the ship *Argus*, only 15 people were alive. Five more died shortly after rescue.
- Two survivors published an account: *The Shipwreck of the Frigate Medusa* by Corréard and Savigny. It became a bestseller and a political scandal — the incompetent captain was seen as a symbol of the corrupt Bourbon regime.

### Géricault's Process (This Is the Insane Part)
- He became obsessed with the story. Decided to paint it at monumental scale.
- **Interviewed the survivors.** Corréard and Savigny sat for him and described everything.
- **Built a scale model of the raft** in his studio.
- **Visited morgues and hospitals** to study dead and dying bodies. He brought severed limbs back to his studio to paint from. His friends described the smell as unbearable.
- **Shaved his head** so he wouldn't be tempted to go out socially. He wanted total isolation and focus.
- Studied the effects of starvation and disease on the human body.
- The painting took about 18 months.

### The Painting
- Enormous: roughly 16 × 23 feet.
- Shows the moment the survivors spot the *Argus* on the horizon. A pyramid of bodies — dead at the bottom, desperate and waving at the top. The ship is a tiny speck.
- It's not Neo-Classical (no idealized bodies, no moral lesson) and it's not quite Romantic yet. It's something new: raw, journalistic, horrifying, and deeply empathetic.
- The Salon reception was mixed. The government was embarrassed (it was an indictment of their incompetence). Critics didn't know what to make of it.
- Géricault took it on tour to London, where it was a sensation.

## What to Emphasize in the Episode
- The research process is the story. The morgue visits, the raft model, the shaved head — this is method acting a century before method acting.
- It's a bridge between Neo-Classicism and Romanticism. The scale and ambition are Neo-Classical. The subject matter and emotional intensity are Romantic.
- Géricault's early death means Delacroix inherited the Romantic mantle. If Géricault had lived, the whole story might be different.
- The political dimension: a painting as an act of journalism and protest.

## Quotes
- Géricault reportedly said of the project: "I will make a painting that will make people tremble."
- On visiting the morgue: he left no direct quotes about this, but contemporary accounts describe the studio as a charnel house.
