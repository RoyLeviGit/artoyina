# J.M.W. Turner (1775–1851)

## Role in the Episode
The British wing of Romanticism. Turner proves this wasn't just a French phenomenon. He's also the painter who most directly anticipates Impressionism and even abstraction — his late works dissolve into pure light and color.

## Key Facts
- Born in London, 1775. Son of a barber. Working-class origins (unusual for a major artist of this era).
- Prodigy. Entered the Royal Academy schools at 14. Full member by 27.
- Traveled obsessively — across Britain, through Europe, especially Italy and Switzerland.
- Became increasingly experimental with age. His early work is detailed and precise. His late work is almost abstract — storms of color with barely recognizable forms.
- Eccentric and secretive. Never married. Lived under a false name in his later years.
- Died in 1851. His will left his entire collection to the British nation (nearly 300 paintings, 30,000 works on paper).

## Key Works for the Episode
- **The Slave Ship** (1840) — Full title: *Slavers Throwing Overboard the Dead and Dying — Typhoon Coming On*. Based on the Zong massacre of 1781. A ship in a blood-red sea, bodies in the water, a storm approaching. It's a painting about the horror of the slave trade rendered as an apocalyptic landscape. Ruskin called it "the noblest sea that Turner ever painted."
- **Rain, Steam and Speed — The Great Western Railway** (1844) — A train hurtling through rain and mist. You can barely see it. The industrial age rendered as a force of nature. A hare runs ahead of the train on the tracks (blink and you miss it).
- **Snow Storm — Steam-Boat off a Harbour's Mouth** (1842) — Turner claimed he had himself lashed to the mast of a ship during a storm to experience it firsthand. Whether this is true is debated, but it's a great story and perfectly captures the Romantic commitment to direct experience.

## What to Emphasize
- Turner as the Romantic who pushed furthest toward abstraction — his late paintings look like they could hang next to a Rothko
- The lashed-to-the-mast story (true or not, it's peak Romanticism)
- Brief — he's a supporting character. 1–2 minutes.

# Caspar David Friedrich (1774–1840)

## Role in the Episode
The German Romantic. If Delacroix is Romanticism as passion and violence, Friedrich is Romanticism as solitude and the sublime. His paintings are quiet, but they hit just as hard.

## Key Facts
- Born in Greifswald, Swedish Pomerania (now Germany), 1774.
- Traumatic childhood: his mother died when he was 7, a sister died when he was 8, and his brother drowned saving him from falling through ice when he was 13. He carried survivor's guilt his entire life.
- Studied at the Copenhagen Academy, then settled in Dresden.
- Deeply religious (Protestant). His landscapes are spiritual experiences — nature as a manifestation of the divine.
- Suffered a stroke in 1835. Largely stopped painting. Died in obscurity in 1840.
- Rediscovered in the early 20th century. Now considered one of the greatest landscape painters ever.

## Key Work
- **Wanderer Above the Sea of Fog** (~1818) — THE Romantic image. A man standing on a rocky peak, back to the viewer, looking out over a vast landscape of fog and mountains. It's been reproduced so many times it's almost a meme, but the original is genuinely powerful.
  - The figure is alone. We can't see his face. We see what he sees.
  - It's about the individual confronting the infinite — the Romantic sublime in one image.
  - Also: *The Abbey in the Oakwood*, *The Sea of Ice*

## What to Emphasize
- The contrast with Delacroix: French Romanticism = passion, violence, color. German Romanticism = solitude, silence, the sublime.
- The *Wanderer* is probably the single most iconic Romantic image. Show it. Let it breathe.
- Brief — 1 minute max. But the image does the work.
