# Eugène Delacroix (1798–1863)

## The Short Version
The anti-David. Where David preached reason, duty, and line, Delacroix unleashed color, emotion, and chaos. He's the central figure of French Romanticism, and his rivalry with Ingres (David's heir) defined the art world for a generation. He also went to North Africa and came back painting like a different person.

## Life Timeline

### Origins & Mystery (1798–1815)
- Born April 26, 1798, in Charenton-Saint-Maurice, near Paris.
- Officially the son of Charles Delacroix, a diplomat. But Charles had a medical condition that likely made him infertile at the time of Eugène's conception. The widely whispered rumor: his real father was **Talleyrand**, the most powerful diplomat in France. Delacroix looked like Talleyrand. Talleyrand quietly protected Delacroix's career. Neither ever confirmed it.
- His father died when he was 7. His mother when he was 16. By 18, he was essentially alone.
- Entered the studio of Pierre-Narcisse Guérin, where he met **Théodore Géricault** — the friendship that would change everything.

### The Breakthrough (1822–1827)
- **The Barque of Dante** (1822) — His Salon debut. Dante and Virgil crossing the river Styx, surrounded by the damned. It's dark, violent, and the flesh of the drowning figures is painted with a visceral intensity that shocked people. He was 24.
- **The Massacre at Chios** (1824) — Greek civilians being slaughtered by Ottoman troops. Based on a real event from the Greek War of Independence. The Salon jury accepted it but critics were divided. One called it "the massacre of painting." Delacroix took that as a compliment.
- Saw Constable's landscapes at the 1824 Salon and was so struck by the Englishman's use of color that he reportedly repainted parts of *Massacre at Chios* in the days before the exhibition opened.
- **The Death of Sardanapalus** (1827) — An Assyrian king watching his palace burn, his concubines and horses being slaughtered, while he reclines on a bed. It's enormous, chaotic, violent, sensual, and completely unhinged. The Salon was horrified. Even Delacroix's supporters were uneasy. This is the painting that most clearly says: I am not David. I am not Ingres. I am something else entirely.

### The Rivalry with Ingres
- This wasn't just personal taste — it was a philosophical war.
- **Ingres** = line, drawing, intellect, restraint, the classical tradition, the Académie
- **Delacroix** = color, paint, emotion, movement, the Romantic tradition, independence
- Ingres reportedly said Delacroix "painted with a drunken broom."
- Delacroix was more diplomatic publicly but privately wrote that Ingres's art was "the complete expression of an incomplete intelligence."
- At one Salon, Ingres allegedly demanded windows be opened after Delacroix's paintings were hung, saying the room smelled of sulfur.
- They were once both appointed to a committee and had to sit next to each other. Witnesses said the tension was unbearable.

### Liberty Leading the People (1830)
- The July Revolution of 1830 — three days of street fighting in Paris that toppled Charles X.
- Delacroix wasn't a revolutionary (unlike David). He was a moderate. But the spectacle moved him.
- He wrote to his brother: "Since I have not fought for the glory of my country, at least I will paint for her."
- **Liberty Leading the People** — Marianne (the female allegory of France) striding over barricades, tricolor in hand, surrounded by fighters from every class. A boy with pistols. A man in a top hat. Dead bodies underfoot.
- It's the most famous political painting of the 19th century. It's on the old French 100-franc note. It's referenced constantly.
- But here's the thing: it's not a documentary. It's a *feeling*. The light is impossible. The composition is theatrical. Delacroix painted the *idea* of revolution, not the reality. That's Romanticism in one image.

### North Africa (1832)
- Invited to join a diplomatic mission to Morocco and Algeria.
- This trip rewired his brain. He filled notebooks with sketches, watercolors, and notes.
- He wrote: "The Greeks and Romans are here at my door, in the Arabs who wrap themselves in a white blanket and look like Cato or Brutus."
- The irony: he went to Africa and saw... ancient Greece. He saw the "noble simplicity" that Winckelmann described, but in living people, not marble statues.
- **Women of Algiers in Their Apartment** (1834) — Painted from memory after visiting a harem. Three women in a richly decorated interior. It's intimate, sensual, and ethnographically detailed. Picasso would later paint 15 variations of this single painting.
- The North Africa work raises questions about Orientalism and the Western gaze that connect directly to Gauguin in Episode 5.

### Late Career & Death (1833–1863)
- Received major government commissions: murals in the Palais Bourbon, the Luxembourg Palace, the Chapel of the Holy Angels at Saint-Sulpice.
- His late murals are extraordinary — looser, more abstract, almost proto-Expressionist.
- Health declined through the 1850s. Tuberculosis.
- Died August 13, 1863, in Paris. His studio contained over 9,000 works.
- His funeral was attended by many of the young painters who would become the Impressionists. They revered him.

## Key Themes for the Episode
1. **Emotion vs. reason** — The fundamental Romantic argument: art should make you *feel*, not just think
2. **Color vs. line** — Not just a technique debate but a worldview debate
3. **The political painting paradox** — *Liberty* is political but Delacroix wasn't really political. He painted the *feeling* of revolution, not its ideology. Compare to David, who was a true believer.
4. **The North Africa trip** — What happens when a European Romantic encounters a non-European culture? Fascination, projection, and the seeds of Orientalism.

## Quotes to Use
- "What moves those of genius, what inspires their work, is not new ideas, but their obsession with the idea that what has already been said is still not enough."
- "The first merit of a painting is to be a feast for the eyes."
- To his brother, 1830: "Since I have not fought for the glory of my country, at least I will paint for her."
- On North Africa: "The Greeks and Romans are here at my door."
- From his journal: "What I require is accuracy for the sake of imagination, not for the sake of itself."
