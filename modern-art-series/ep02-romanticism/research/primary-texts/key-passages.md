## 2. Delacroix's Journal

### On Painting
> "The first merit of a painting is to be a feast for the eyes."

> "What moves those of genius, what inspires their work, is not new ideas, but their obsession with the idea that what has already been said is still not enough."

### On North Africa (1832)
> "The Greeks and Romans are here at my door, in the Arabs who wrap themselves in a white blanket and look like Cato or Brutus."

> "I am quite overwhelmed by what I have seen. I am like a man dreaming, who sees things he is afraid to see escape him."

### On the Rivalry (private journal)
> Delacroix on Ingres: "the complete expression of an incomplete intelligence"
> (Use carefully — this is sharp and personal)

### On Liberty Leading the People
> To his brother, October 1830: "Since I have not fought for the glory of my country, at least I will paint for her."

### On His Own Method
> "What I require is accuracy for the sake of imagination, not for the sake of itself."

**Source:** *The Journal of Eugène Delacroix*, translated by Walter Pach (various editions). Also available in selections online.

## 3. Corréard & Savigny — "The Shipwreck of the Frigate Medusa" (1817)

### Why It Matters
This is the source text for Géricault's painting. Two survivors published this account, and it became a political scandal. Géricault read it, interviewed the authors, and painted the result.

### Key Passages (paraphrased for the script — the original is very long)
- The raft was built in haste. 147 people crammed onto it. It was barely above water.
- The tow ropes from the lifeboats were cut. The raft was set adrift.
- By the second night, fights broke out. Men went mad. Some threw themselves into the sea.
- By day four, the survivors began eating the dead.
- After 13 days, the brig *Argus* spotted the raft. 15 survivors remained. 5 more died after rescue.

### For the Script
You don't need to read from this text directly, but knowing the details lets you narrate the story vividly. The key beats: the incompetent captain, the cutting of the ropes, the descent into madness, the cannibalism, the rescue. Then pivot to Géricault's obsessive process.

**Source:** Available in English translation. Also summarized well in Jonathan Miles's *The Wreck of the Medusa* (2007).

## 4. Edmund Burke — "A Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of Our Ideas of the Sublime and Beautiful" (1757)

### The Key Idea
> "Whatever is fitted in any sort to excite the ideas of pain and danger... is a source of the sublime; that is, it is productive of the strongest emotion which the mind is capable of feeling."

> "The passion caused by the great and sublime in nature... is astonishment, and astonishment is that state of the soul in which all its motions are suspended, with some degree of horror."

### Why It Matters
Burke gave Romanticism its philosophical vocabulary. The sublime = awe + terror. This is what Friedrich paints. This is what Turner chases. This is the emotional register that separates Romanticism from everything before it.

### For the Script
You probably don't need to quote Burke directly, but the concept of the sublime should be explained in plain language. Something like: "There's a word for that feeling you get standing at the edge of a cliff — when you're terrified and amazed at the same time. The Romantics called it the sublime. And they built an entire art movement around chasing it."
