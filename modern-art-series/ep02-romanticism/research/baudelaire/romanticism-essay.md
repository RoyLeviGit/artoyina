# Charles Baudelaire — "What is Romanticism?" (from the Salon of 1846)

## Why He's in This Episode
Baudelaire wasn't a painter — he was a poet and critic. But he understood Romanticism better than most of the Romantics themselves. His Salon review of 1846 contains the clearest, most quotable definition of what Romanticism actually was. He's the poet who explained the painters to the world.

## Key Passages

### The Definition
> "Romanticism is precisely situated neither in choice of subjects nor in exact truth, but in a mode of feeling."

> "They looked for it outside themselves, but it was only to be found within."

> "For me, Romanticism is the most recent, the most current expression of the beautiful."

> "Whoever says Romanticism says modern art — that is, intimacy, spirituality, color, aspiration towards the infinite, expressed by every means available to the arts."

### On Delacroix
Baudelaire was Delacroix's greatest champion. He wrote about him repeatedly across decades.

> "Delacroix is the most suggestive of all painters, the one whose works... make one think the most, and recall to the memory the greatest number of poetic feelings and thoughts."

> "He is the painter who has best expressed the modern idea of beauty."

### On Color vs. Line
> "There are two kinds of colorists: those who use color as a means of expressing an idea, and those who use it as a means of expressing a sensation. Delacroix belongs to the second category."

## What to Use in the Script
- The "mode of feeling" quote is the one. Put it on screen. It's the most concise definition of Romanticism you'll find.
- Baudelaire as a character: briefly introduce him as the poet who "understood the painters better than they understood themselves" (from your series notes). You don't need his full biography — just position him as the movement's interpreter.
- His championing of Delacroix gives you a way to articulate what Delacroix was doing without it being purely your own analysis — you can say "even at the time, the smartest critic in Paris saw what was happening."

## Source
- Full text of the Salon of 1846 is available in various translations. Jonathan Mayne's translation in *Art in Paris 1845–1862* (Phaidon) is the standard English version.
- Also available on various academic and public domain sites.
