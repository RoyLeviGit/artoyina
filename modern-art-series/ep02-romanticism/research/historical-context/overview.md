# Historical Context: Romanticism (~1815–1850)

## What Was Happening in the World

### Post-Napoleon Europe
- Napoleon defeated at Waterloo (1815). The Congress of Vienna redraws the map of Europe.
- The Bourbon monarchy restored in France. Conservative backlash across the continent.
- But the revolutionary genie is out of the bottle. Uprisings and independence movements everywhere:
  - Greek War of Independence (1821–1829) — directly inspired Delacroix's *Massacre at Chios*
  - July Revolution in France (1830) — inspired *Liberty Leading the People*
  - Revolutions of 1848 across Europe
- The tension: governments trying to restore the old order vs. populations who've tasted revolution.

### The Industrial Revolution
- Accelerating through this period. Factories, railways, urbanization.
- Turner's *Rain, Steam and Speed* captures this — the train as a force of nature.
- Romanticism is partly a reaction *against* industrialization — a longing for nature, emotion, the sublime, in a world increasingly dominated by machines and commerce.

### Colonialism & Orientalism
- European powers expanding into North Africa, the Middle East, Asia.
- Delacroix's trip to Morocco/Algeria (1832) happens in this context — France had invaded Algeria in 1830.
- The "Orient" becomes a source of fascination for European artists: exotic, sensual, "other."
- This is the beginning of the Orientalist tradition that Edward Said would critique in the 20th century (connects to Gauguin in Episode 5).

### Intellectual Climate
- The Enlightenment's faith in reason is being questioned. Emotion, intuition, the irrational are being rehabilitated.
- Key thinkers: Rousseau (earlier, but hugely influential), the Schlegel brothers, Schelling, Hegel.
- In literature: Byron, Shelley, Keats, Hugo, Goethe. The Romantic poets and novelists are doing in words what the painters are doing in images.
- The concept of the **genius** — the individual who transcends society, who suffers for their vision — is a Romantic invention. It's the origin of the "tortured artist" myth that persists to this day.

## The Art World Context

### The Line vs. Color Debate
- This is THE debate of the period. It goes back centuries (Poussin vs. Rubens in the 17th century) but reaches its peak with Ingres vs. Delacroix.
- **Line (dessin):** Drawing is the foundation of art. Form, contour, precision. Art appeals to the intellect. Champions: Ingres, the Académie.
- **Color (coloris):** Color is the soul of painting. Sensation, emotion, movement. Art appeals to the senses. Champions: Delacroix, the Romantics.
- This isn't just a technique argument — it's about what art *is for*. Should it educate or move you? Should it be controlled or free?

### The Salon (Still)
- Still the dominant institution. Still the only real path to recognition.
- But cracks are forming. The jury is conservative. Romantic works are controversial.
- Géricault's *Raft of the Medusa* was accepted but made the government uncomfortable.
- Delacroix's *Sardanapalus* was so controversial it nearly ended his career.
- The tension between the Salon system and artistic freedom will eventually explode in Episodes 3 and 4.

### The Sublime
- A key Romantic concept. Edmund Burke defined it in 1757: the sublime is the experience of awe mixed with terror in the face of something vast, powerful, or incomprehensible.
- Mountains, storms, oceans, ruins — anything that makes you feel small.
- Friedrich's *Wanderer Above the Sea of Fog* = the sublime in one image.
- Turner's storms = the sublime as paint.
- This is different from beauty. Beauty is pleasant. The sublime is overwhelming.

## The Big Question for the Episode
Neo-Classicism said: art should appeal to your mind. It should teach virtue. It should be rational.

Romanticism says: art should appeal to your gut. It should make you *feel*. Reason isn't enough.

The question: **Should art appeal to your mind or your gut?** And is that even the right way to frame it — or is the best art the kind that does both?
