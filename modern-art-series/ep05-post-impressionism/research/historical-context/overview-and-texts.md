# Supporting Artists — Episode 5

## Georges Seurat (1859–1891)
- The scientist of the group. Took Chevreul's color theory (from Episode 4) and made it systematic.
- **Pointillism / Neo-Impressionism:** tiny dots of pure color placed side by side. The eye mixes them at a distance.
- **A Sunday Afternoon on the Island of La Grande Jatte** (1884–86) — Enormous. Parisians relaxing by the Seine. Every figure is still, geometric, almost frozen. It took two years to complete. It's Impressionism made monumental and mathematical.
- Died at 31 (meningitis or diphtheria). Like Géricault, a career cut brutally short.
- For the episode: Seurat is the bridge between Impressionism and Post-Impressionism. He took Impressionist color science to its logical extreme. 2–3 minutes.

## Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec (1864–1901)
- Born into aristocracy. Broke both legs as a teenager (genetic bone condition). They never grew properly — he was 4'8" as an adult.
- Immersed himself in Montmartre nightlife: cabarets, brothels, dance halls.
- His posters for the Moulin Rouge are iconic — bold, graphic, influenced by Japanese prints.
- Painted sex workers with empathy and without judgment. He lived among them.
- Alcoholism destroyed him. Died at 36.
- For the episode: brief — 1 minute. He represents the Post-Impressionist engagement with modern urban life (vs. Cézanne's mountains and Gauguin's tropics).

# Historical Context: Post-Impressionism (~1880–1906)

## The Term
- "Post-Impressionism" was coined by the critic Roger Fry in 1910 for an exhibition in London. It's a retrospective label — none of these artists called themselves Post-Impressionists.
- It's a catch-all for artists who came after Impressionism and went beyond it in different directions. There's no single Post-Impressionist style — that's the point.

## The Art Market
- The dealer system is replacing the Salon. Figures like Paul Durand-Ruel (Impressionists), Ambroise Vollard (Cézanne, Gauguin), and Theo van Gogh (at Boussod, Valadon & Cie) are the new gatekeepers.
- But the market is tiny. Cézanne sold almost nothing until his 50s. Van Gogh sold one painting in his lifetime (maybe two — scholars debate). Gauguin was perpetually broke.

## Colonialism & Primitivism
- European empires at their peak. The "Scramble for Africa." France controls Algeria, Tunisia, much of West Africa, Indochina, Tahiti.
- "Primitivism" in art: European artists looking to non-Western cultures for inspiration. African masks, Japanese prints, Polynesian mythology.
- The problem: this "inspiration" happens within a power structure. Europeans take what they want from colonized cultures and reframe it as their own artistic breakthrough.
- Gauguin is the most extreme example, but the pattern is widespread.

## The Bridge to the 20th Century
- Cézanne → Cubism (Picasso, Braque)
- Van Gogh → Expressionism (Die Brücke, Fauvism)
- Gauguin → Symbolism, Primitivism, Fauvism
- Seurat → geometric abstraction, Op Art
- These three (plus Seurat) are the launchpad for everything that happens in 20th-century art.

# Primary Texts — Episode 5

## Edward Said — "Orientalism" (1978)
### Key Idea
The "Orient" is a European invention — a way of defining the East as exotic, irrational, sensual, and inferior, in contrast to a rational, civilized West. This framework justified colonialism and shaped how Europeans represented non-Western cultures in art, literature, and scholarship.

### Relevant to Gauguin
Gauguin's Tahiti is a textbook case of Orientalist projection. He went looking for a primitive paradise and "found" it — because he constructed it. The real Tahiti (colonized, Christianized, modernizing) didn't match his fantasy, so he painted the fantasy instead.

### For the Script
You don't need to lecture on Said. Just name the dynamic: "A European man goes to a colonized land, takes what he wants, and paints a fantasy of paradise. There's a word for that pattern. The scholar Edward Said called it Orientalism."

## Cézanne's Conversations with Émile Bernard (1904–1906)
### Key Passages
> "Treat nature by the cylinder, the sphere, the cone, everything brought into proper perspective so that each side of an object or a plane is directed towards a central point."

> "I owe you the truth in painting, and I will tell it to you."

> "With an apple I will astonish Paris."

> On Monet: "Monet is only an eye, but my God, what an eye!"

**Source:** Bernard published these conversations after Cézanne's death. Available in various art history anthologies.
