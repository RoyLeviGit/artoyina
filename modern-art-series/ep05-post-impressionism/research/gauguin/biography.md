# Paul Gauguin (1848–1903)

## The Short Version
The stockbroker who abandoned his wife and five children to become a painter, then abandoned Europe entirely to "find the primitive" in Tahiti. His paintings are extraordinary. His life is deeply problematic. He's the artist who forces you to confront the question: can you separate the art from the person?

## Life Timeline

### The Stockbroker (1848–1883)
- Born June 7, 1848, in Paris. His mother was half-Peruvian (her family had aristocratic connections in Lima). Spent part of his childhood in Peru.
- Became a successful stockbroker in Paris. Married Mette-Sophie Gad, a Danish woman. Five children.
- Collected Impressionist paintings as a hobby. Started painting on Sundays.
- Met Pissarro, who encouraged him. Exhibited with the Impressionists in 1879, 1880, 1881, 1882, and 1886.
- The stock market crashed in 1882. Lost his job. Decided to become a full-time painter.
- His wife took the children and moved back to Denmark. Gauguin followed briefly, then returned to Paris. He essentially abandoned his family. Mette never forgave him.

### Brittany & Pont-Aven (1886–1890)
- Moved to Pont-Aven in Brittany, seeking a "primitive" culture closer to home.
- Developed Synthetism / Cloisonnism: bold outlines, flat areas of color, simplified forms. Influenced by Japanese prints, medieval stained glass, and folk art.
- **Vision After the Sermon (Jacob Wrestling with the Angel)** (1888) — Breton women in white caps watching a biblical vision. The ground is bright red. There's no perspective, no depth — just flat, bold color. This is the break with Impressionism. Gauguin isn't painting what he sees. He's painting what the women *believe*.
- The Arles episode with Van Gogh (October–December 1888). Nine weeks. Arguments. The ear. Gauguin left.

### Tahiti — First Trip (1891–1893)
- Sailed to Tahiti in April 1891. He was 43.
- His stated goal: to escape European civilization and find an "unspoiled" culture.
- The reality: Tahiti had been a French colony since 1880. Papeete (the capital) had a cathedral, a governor's mansion, and French colonial infrastructure. The "primitive paradise" Gauguin sought was largely a fantasy.
- He moved to a rural area. Took a 13-year-old Tahitian girl, Teha'amana, as his "wife." She was one of several very young local women he had relationships with during his time in the Pacific. By any modern standard, this is sexual exploitation of minors in a colonial context.
- Painted prolifically. The colors intensified. The subjects: Tahitian women, mythology, landscape.
- **Spirit of the Dead Watching (Manao tupapau)** (1892) — A young Tahitian woman lying face-down on a bed, a dark spirit behind her. Gauguin said it was inspired by Teha'amana's fear of ghosts. It's a powerful painting. It's also a European man painting a naked teenage girl and framing it through his own interpretation of her culture.
- Returned to Paris in 1893. Tried to sell the Tahitian paintings. Limited success.

### Tahiti & the Marquesas — Final Years (1895–1903)
- Returned to Tahiti in 1895. Never came back to Europe.
- Health deteriorated: syphilis, a broken ankle that never healed, heart problems.
- **Where Do We Come From? What Are We? Where Are We Going?** (1897–98) — His largest and most ambitious painting. A frieze-like composition read right to left: birth, life, death. Tahitian figures in a lush landscape. He claimed he painted it in a frenzy before a suicide attempt (the suicide attempt may have been staged or exaggerated for dramatic effect — Gauguin was a skilled self-mythologizer).
- Moved to the Marquesas Islands in 1901. Continued painting. Clashed with colonial and church authorities.
- Died May 8, 1903, in Atuona, Marquesas Islands. Alone, sick, in legal trouble.

## The Orientalism Problem
- Gauguin's Tahitian work is inseparable from colonialism. He went to a colonized land, took what he wanted (including underage girls), and constructed a fantasy of "primitive" paradise that served his artistic needs.
- Edward Said's *Orientalism* (1978) provides the framework: the "Orient" (or in this case, the Pacific) as a European projection — exotic, sensual, timeless, available.
- This doesn't mean the paintings aren't powerful. They are. But you can't discuss them honestly without discussing the context.
- The question for the script: how do you hold both truths? The paintings are extraordinary. The man was exploitative. These aren't contradictions — they're the same story.

## Key Themes
1. **The flight from civilization** — What drives a man to abandon everything? Genuine spiritual seeking? Selfishness? Both?
2. **Primitivism** — The European fantasy of the "noble savage." Gauguin didn't find an unspoiled paradise — he projected one.
3. **Art from exploitation** — The paintings are made possible by colonial power dynamics. The beauty is built on an ugly foundation.
4. **Imagination over observation** — Gauguin's break with Impressionism: don't paint what you see, paint what you dream, remember, believe.

## Quotes to Use

- "Life being what it is, one dreams of revenge."
- "I have decided on Tahiti... and I hope to cultivate my art there in the wild and primitive state."
- "It is the eye of ignorance that assigns a fixed and unchangeable color to every object."
- "Where do we come from? What are we? Where are we going?"
