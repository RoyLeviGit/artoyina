# Paul Cézanne (1839–1906)

## The Short Version
The recluse who accidentally invented modern art. He painted the same mountain 80 times, the same apples over and over, and in doing so figured out something about the structure of seeing that led directly to Cubism. Picasso called him "the father of us all."

## Life Timeline

### Early Years & Friendship with Zola (1839–1861)
- Born January 19, 1839, in Aix-en-Provence. Son of a wealthy banker.
- Childhood best friend: Émile Zola, the future novelist. They were inseparable. This friendship — and its devastating end — is one of the great stories in art history.
- His father wanted him to be a lawyer. Cézanne studied law reluctantly, painted obsessively.
- Finally convinced his father to let him go to Paris in 1861.

### Paris & Failure (1861–1870s)
- Failed the entrance exam for the École des Beaux-Arts.
- His early work is dark, violent, thickly painted — nothing like what he'd become. Murders, orgies, portraits slathered with a palette knife. The Salon rejected him repeatedly.
- Met Pissarro, who became his mentor. Pissarro taught him to lighten his palette, to paint outdoors, to look.
- Exhibited with the Impressionists in 1874 and 1877. His work was the most mocked in the shows. Critics singled him out as the worst of the group.
- Gradually withdrew from Paris. Returned to Aix.

### The Breakthrough — "Treating Nature by the Cylinder, the Sphere, the Cone" (1880s–1890s)
- This is where Cézanne becomes Cézanne.
- He realized that Impressionism had a problem: it captured the surface of things beautifully but lost their solidity. A Monet haystack shimmers with light but doesn't feel like you could touch it.
- Cézanne wanted both: the color and light of Impressionism AND the structure and weight of the Old Masters.
- His solution: break everything down into geometric forms. A mountain is a cone. An apple is a sphere. A tree trunk is a cylinder. Then rebuild the image from these forms using color.
- **Mont Sainte-Victoire** — He painted this mountain outside Aix over 80 times across 25 years. Each painting is different. He's not painting the mountain — he's painting the *act of seeing* the mountain. Multiple viewpoints, shifting planes, colors that build form.
- **The Card Players** (1890–95) — Five versions. Two peasants playing cards. Monumental, still, geometric. They look like they're carved from stone.
- **Still Life with Apples** (various) — His still lifes are deceptively radical. The table tilts. The perspective shifts. Objects are seen from multiple angles simultaneously. This is what Picasso and Braque would take and explode into Cubism.

### The Break with Zola (1886)
- Zola published *L'Œuvre* (The Masterpiece), a novel about a failed painter who can never realize his vision and eventually kills himself.
- The character was transparently based on Cézanne. Everyone knew it.
- Cézanne wrote Zola a brief, polite thank-you note for the book. They never spoke again.
- The pain of this break haunted Cézanne for the rest of his life. He reportedly wept when Zola died in 1902.

### The Late Work & Recognition (1895–1906)
- The dealer Ambroise Vollard gave Cézanne his first solo exhibition in 1895. He was 56. The younger generation of painters was stunned.
- Émile Bernard visited him in Aix and recorded his conversations. These are our best source for Cézanne's ideas.
- **The Large Bathers** (1898–1905) — His final masterpiece. Nude figures under trees, arranged in a triangular composition. The bodies are barely bodies — they're geometric forms integrated into the landscape. It's not about nudity. It's about structure.
- Died October 22, 1906, in Aix. He'd been painting outdoors, got caught in a storm, collapsed. Pneumonia.
- The year after his death, a major retrospective at the Salon d'Automne in Paris. Picasso, Braque, Matisse, and an entire generation saw it and understood: this man had shown them the future.

## Key Themes
1. **Structure beneath surface** — Cézanne's answer to Impressionism: light isn't enough. You need to find the bones of things.
2. **Obsessive repetition** — The mountain, the apples, the card players. Painting the same thing until you understand it.
3. **The Zola break** — Art and friendship. The cost of being seen (and misunderstood) by someone who knows you.
4. **The father of modern art** — Not a title he sought. He just painted what he saw. But what he saw was the future.

## Quotes to Use
- "Treat nature by the cylinder, the sphere, the cone, everything brought into proper perspective."
- "With an apple I will astonish Paris."
- "I am the primitive of the way I have discovered."
- On Monet: "Monet is only an eye, but my God, what an eye!"
- To Émile Bernard: "I owe you the truth in painting, and I will tell it to you."
- Picasso on Cézanne: "He was my one and only master... Cézanne was the father of us all."
