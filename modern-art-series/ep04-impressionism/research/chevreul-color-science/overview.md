# Chevreul's Color Science — Episode 4

## Michel Eugène Chevreul (1786–1889)

### Who He Was
A chemist, not an artist. Director of dyeing at the Gobelins tapestry manufactory in Paris. He lived to be 102.

### The Discovery
- Workers at the Gobelins complained that certain dye colors looked wrong when placed next to each other — blacks looked different next to blues than next to reds.
- Chevreul realized the problem wasn't the dyes. It was the eye. **Colors change depending on what's next to them.**

### The Law of Simultaneous Contrast of Colors (1839)
Published as *De la loi du contraste simultané des couleurs*.

Key principles (simplified for the script):
1. **Complementary colors intensify each other.** Put red next to green and both look more vivid. Put blue next to orange — same effect.
2. **Colors "push" neighboring colors toward their complement.** A grey square on a red background will look slightly greenish. The same grey on a blue background will look slightly orangish.
3. **Shadows aren't just darker versions of a color — they contain the complement.** A shadow on a yellow object isn't just dark yellow — it has violet in it.

### Why This Matters for Impressionism
- The Impressionists (especially Monet, Pissarro, and later Seurat) used these principles consciously.
- Instead of mixing colors on the palette (which dulls them), they placed pure colors side by side on the canvas and let the viewer's eye do the mixing. This is why Impressionist paintings look so vibrant up close — you see individual strokes of pure color — and then resolve into a coherent image from a distance.
- Monet's *Haystacks*: the shadows aren't brown or black. They're purple, blue, orange. Because that's what Chevreul's science says they should be. And that's what the eye actually sees if you look carefully enough.

### For the Script
Explain it simply. Something like: "A chemist named Chevreul figured out something that changed painting forever: colors aren't fixed. They change depending on what's next to them. Put blue next to orange and both explode. The Impressionists took that science and ran with it."

You could demonstrate this visually: show two identical grey squares on different colored backgrounds. The viewer will see them as different colors. That's Chevreul's law in action.

# Historical Context: Impressionism (~1860–1886)

## What Was Happening in the World

### Haussmann's Paris
- Baron Haussmann redesigned Paris under Napoleon III (1853–1870).
- Medieval streets demolished. Wide boulevards, uniform buildings, parks, sewers.
- The Paris the Impressionists painted — the cafés, the boulevards, the parks, the train stations — was brand new. They were painting modernity as it was being built.
- But Haussmann's project also displaced the poor and was designed partly to prevent barricades (wide streets are harder to block than narrow ones). The beautiful new Paris had an authoritarian logic.

### Modern Life
- Railways connecting Paris to the countryside. The Impressionists could take a train to Argenteuil or Giverny and paint.
- Leisure culture: boating, picnics, cafés, dance halls, the opera. This is what Renoir and Degas painted.
- Department stores, gas lighting, photography — the texture of modern urban life.
- Baudelaire (again) had called for a "painter of modern life" in 1863. The Impressionists answered.

### Japonisme
- Japan opened to Western trade in the 1850s after centuries of isolation.
- Japanese prints (ukiyo-e) flooded into Paris. Artists were stunned by them: flat color, bold outlines, asymmetric compositions, everyday subjects.
- Influence on: Degas (cropped compositions), Cassatt (her prints), Monet (the Japanese bridge at Giverny), Van Gogh (Episode 5).
- This is a two-way cultural exchange, though the power dynamics were unequal.

### The Invention of Portable Paint Tubes
- Before the 1840s, artists ground their own pigments and stored paint in pig bladders. Painting outdoors was impractical.
- The collapsible metal paint tube (invented ~1841) made plein air painting possible.
- Renoir reportedly said: "Without tubes of paint, there would have been no Impressionism."
- It's a small technological detail with enormous consequences.

## The Big Question for the Episode
Is a painting "finished" if you can see the brushstrokes?

The Académie said no. A finished painting should have a smooth, invisible surface. The Impressionists said: the brushstroke IS the point. It captures the moment, the light, the act of seeing. Finish is a lie — the world doesn't hold still.
