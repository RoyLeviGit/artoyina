# Claude Monet (1840–1926)

## The Short Version
The man who gave Impressionism its name (accidentally) and spent his entire life chasing one thing: light. He started as a broke rebel painting outdoors and ended as a national treasure painting water lilies in a garden he designed himself. His career *is* the arc of Impressionism.

## Life Timeline

### Early Years (1840–1862)
- Born in Paris, November 14, 1840. Family moved to Le Havre (Normandy coast) when he was five.
- Started as a caricaturist — actually made money selling caricatures as a teenager.
- Met Eugène Boudin, a local painter who worked outdoors (en plein air). Boudin dragged him outside to paint. Monet later said: "It was as if a veil was torn from my eyes. I understood what painting could be."
- Went to Paris to study. Refused to follow the academic curriculum. Met Renoir, Sisley, and Bazille in Charles Gleyre's studio — the core of the future Impressionist group.

### The Struggle (1862–1873)
- Repeatedly rejected by the Salon. When accepted, his work was mocked or ignored.
- Desperately poor for most of this period. Begged friends for money. Couldn't afford paint.
- Married Camille Doncieux in 1870. She modeled for many of his paintings.
- Franco-Prussian War: fled to London (1870–71), where he saw Turner's paintings. The influence is debated but the timing is suggestive.
- Moved to Argenteuil (a suburb on the Seine) in 1871. This became the Impressionist laboratory — Renoir, Sisley, and Manet all came to paint there.

### Impression, Sunrise (1872)
- Painted at Le Havre. A harbor at dawn. Boats, water, fog, and a blazing orange sun reflected on the surface. It's loose, fast, almost sketchy.
- Exhibited at the first independent exhibition in 1874.
- Critic Louis Leroy reviewed the show mockingly. He titled his review "Exhibition of the Impressionists" — using Monet's title as an insult. The name stuck.
- The original review: "Impression — I was certain of it. I was just telling myself that, since I was impressed, there had to be some impression in it... and what freedom, what ease of workmanship! Wallpaper in its embryonic state is more finished than that seascape."

### The Independent Exhibitions (1874–1886)
- Eight exhibitions total (1874, 1876, 1877, 1879, 1880, 1881, 1882, 1886).
- Monet participated in five of them. The group was never stable — constant arguments about who to include, whether to submit to the Salon simultaneously, money.
- These exhibitions are the direct descendants of Courbet's Pavilion of Realism. The principle: we don't need the Salon.

### Personal Tragedy
- Camille died in 1879. She was 32. Monet was at her bedside. He later admitted that even as she lay dying, he found himself studying the colors of death on her face — the blue, the grey, the yellow. He was horrified at himself. He painted her on her deathbed.
- "Before I even thought of recording those features to which I was so deeply attached, my organism was already reacting to the color sensations."
- This is one of the most revealing and disturbing things any artist has ever said about their own process.

### Giverny & The Series Paintings (1883–1926)
- Moved to Giverny in 1883. Married Alice Hoschedé (1892).
- Built the garden. Designed the water lily pond. The Japanese bridge. He literally constructed his own subject matter.
- **The Series Paintings** — This is where Monet becomes something more than an Impressionist:
  - *Haystacks* (1890–91) — 25 paintings of the same haystacks at different times of day and seasons. The subject doesn't matter. The light does.
  - *Rouen Cathedral* (1892–94) — 30+ paintings of the same cathedral facade. Morning, noon, sunset, fog. The stone dissolves into color.
  - *Water Lilies* (1896–1926) — Hundreds of paintings. The late ones are enormous, almost abstract. No horizon line. No sky. Just water, reflections, and flowers.
- His eyesight deteriorated (cataracts). The late paintings get increasingly blurry, the colors shift. He destroyed many canvases he was unhappy with.
- The Orangerie murals: eight massive Water Lily panels installed in two oval rooms in Paris. Monet donated them to France. They were installed after his death. Walking into those rooms is one of the great art experiences in the world.
- Died December 5, 1926, at Giverny. He was 86.

## Key Themes for the Episode
1. **Seeing vs. knowing** — Monet painted what the eye actually sees (shifting light, dissolved forms) rather than what the mind knows is there (solid objects, fixed colors). This is the Impressionist revolution in one idea.
2. **The series concept** — Painting the same thing over and over to capture how light changes it. The subject is irrelevant; perception is everything.
3. **The deathbed confession** — Studying color on his dying wife's face. The artist's eye as both gift and curse.
4. **From rebel to institution** — He started rejected and broke. He ended as a national treasure with a garden designed for painting.

## Quotes to Use
- On Boudin: "It was as if a veil was torn from my eyes."
- On Camille's death: "Before I even thought of recording those features... my organism was already reacting to the color sensations."
- "I would like to paint the way a bird sings."
- "Color is my day-long obsession, joy, and torment."
- On the Haystacks: "For me, a landscape does not exist in its own right, since its appearance changes at every moment."
