# Mary Cassatt (1844–1926)

## Role in the Episode
The strong secondary focus. An American woman who became one of the most important Impressionists — and whose story illuminates everything the movement was about: independence, breaking from institutions, seeing the world differently. She also connects the movement to America and to feminism.

## Life Timeline

### An American in Paris
- Born May 22, 1844, in Allegheny City, Pennsylvania (now part of Pittsburgh). Wealthy family. Her father was a stockbroker.
- Traveled Europe as a child. Decided to become an artist at 15.
- Studied at the Pennsylvania Academy of the Fine Arts. Frustrated by the limited opportunities for women — they couldn't draw from nude models, couldn't access the same training as men.
- Moved to Paris in 1866. Studied privately (women couldn't attend the École des Beaux-Arts).
- Exhibited at the Salon from 1868. Had some success but felt constrained by the jury system.

### Meeting Degas & Joining the Impressionists
- Saw Degas's pastels in a gallery window in 1875. She later said: "I used to go and flatten my nose against that window and absorb all I could of his art. It changed my life. I saw art then as I wanted to see it."
- Degas invited her to exhibit with the Impressionists in 1877. She accepted immediately.
- She later wrote: "I accepted with joy. At last I could work with absolute independence without considering the opinion of a jury. I had already recognized who were my true masters. I admired Manet, Courbet, and Degas. I hated conventional art."
- She exhibited in four of the eight Impressionist exhibitions (1879, 1880, 1881, 1886).

### Her Art
- Specialized in intimate domestic scenes: mothers and children, women at tea, women reading, women at the theater.
- This sounds conventional, but it wasn't. She painted these scenes from the *inside* — from the woman's perspective. The viewer is in the room, not looking in from outside.
- **The Child's Bath** (1893) — A mother bathing a child. Seen from above, an unusual angle. The intimacy is physical — you can almost feel the water. Influenced by Japanese prints (the flat planes, the bold outlines).
- **Little Girl in a Blue Armchair** (1878) — A child sprawled in an oversized chair, bored and restless. It's completely natural. No posing, no sentimentality. Degas reportedly helped with the background.
- **The Letter** (1890–91) — A color print (aquatint/drypoint). Part of a series of 10 prints inspired by Japanese ukiyo-e. These prints are technically extraordinary and among her finest works.
- Her printmaking is often overlooked but it's remarkable — she pushed the medium further than almost any of her contemporaries.

### Feminism & Independence
- Never married. Chose art over domesticity — a radical choice for a woman of her class and era.
- Financially independent (family money + sales). This gave her freedom most women artists didn't have.
- Actively promoted other women artists. Helped American collectors (especially the Havemeyers) build their Impressionist collections — she's a major reason American museums have so many Impressionist paintings today.
- Her letters are remarkable documents. Sharp, opinionated, funny, and deeply aware of the constraints she faced as a woman.

### Later Years
- Eyesight failed (like Monet). Stopped painting around 1914.
- Died June 14, 1926, at Château de Beaufresne, near Paris. Same year as Monet.

## Key Themes for the Episode
1. **Independence** — Her story embodies the Impressionist ethos: reject the jury, work on your own terms
2. **The female gaze** — She painted women's lives from the inside. In a movement dominated by men painting women (Renoir's nudes, Degas's dancers), she offered the view from the other side.
3. **The American connection** — She bridged Paris and America, and her influence on American collecting shaped what we see in museums today
4. **Japanese influence** — Her prints show how deeply Japanese art influenced the Impressionists (connects to the broader Japonisme trend)

## Quotes to Use

- On joining the Impressionists: "I accepted with joy. At last I could work with absolute independence without considering the opinion of a jury."
