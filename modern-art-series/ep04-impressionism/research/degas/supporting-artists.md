# Supporting Artists — Episode 4: Impressionism

---

## Edgar Degas (1834–1917)
- The Impressionist who didn't really like being called an Impressionist. He preferred "Realist" or "Independent."
- Rarely painted outdoors. His subjects: ballet dancers, horse races, laundresses, women bathing.
- Master draftsman — closer to Ingres than to Monet in technique. But his compositions are radical: cropped figures, unusual angles, asymmetry inspired by photography and Japanese prints.
- Key works: *The Dance Class* (1874), *L'Absinthe* (1876), *The Tub* (1886)
- Difficult personality. Increasingly antisemitic in later life (the Dreyfus Affair). This is uncomfortable but shouldn't be erased.
- Connection to Cassatt: he invited her into the group. They had a complex, intense friendship/rivalry.
- For the episode: 1–2 minutes. Show *The Dance Class* and note how the composition (off-center, cropped) is as revolutionary as Monet's color.

---

## Pierre-Auguste Renoir (1841–1919)
- The Impressionist who loved beauty. Where Monet chased light and Degas chased movement, Renoir chased pleasure.
- Key works: *Bal du moulin de la Galette* (1876) — a dance in Montmartre, dappled sunlight, joy. *Luncheon of the Boating Party* (1881).
- Later turned away from Impressionism toward a more classical style ("Ingres period"). His late nudes are controversial — lush, rosy, and to modern eyes, uncomfortably objectifying.
- For the episode: brief mention. He represents the "joyful" side of Impressionism.

---

## Berthe Morisot (1841–1895)
- The other major woman Impressionist (alongside Cassatt).
- Married Eugène Manet (Édouard's brother). Exhibited in seven of the eight Impressionist exhibitions — more than any other member.
- Painted domestic scenes, gardens, women and children. Her brushwork is among the loosest and most daring in the group.
- Key work: *The Cradle* (1872) — a mother watching her sleeping baby through a veil of gauze. Intimate, tender, technically bold.
- For the episode: mention alongside Cassatt. The fact that two of the most committed Impressionists were women is worth noting.

---

## Camille Pissarro (1830–1903)
- The elder statesman. Oldest of the group. The only one who exhibited in all eight independent exhibitions.
- Born in the Danish West Indies (now US Virgin Islands) to a Sephardic Jewish family. The most politically radical of the Impressionists — an anarchist.
- Mentored nearly everyone: Cézanne, Gauguin, Signac, Seurat all learned from him.
- Key works: landscapes of Pontoise and Éragny, later cityscapes of Paris.
- For the episode: mention as the glue that held the group together. "The humble and colossal Pissarro" (Cézanne's words).

---

## Édouard Manet (1832–1883)
- Technically not an Impressionist — he never exhibited with them. But he's the bridge figure.
- *Le Déjeuner sur l'herbe* (1863) and *Olympia* (1863) — both shown at the Salon des Refusés. Both scandalized Paris. A nude woman staring directly at the viewer, unapologetic. This is the moment the old order cracked.
- The Impressionists revered him. He influenced them all but maintained his independence.
- For the episode: mention in the "what came before" section. Manet opened the door; the Impressionists walked through it.
