# Episode 4 Outline: "The Rebels Who Painted Light"
## Movement: Impressionism | Runtime Target: 40–50 min

---

### 1. COLD OPEN (2–3 min)
**Scene:** Paris, April 15, 1874. A photographer's studio on the Boulevard des Capucines. A group of painters — rejected by the Salon, broke, desperate — have pooled their money to rent the space and hang their own show. 165 works. No jury. No prizes. No permission.

A critic walks in, looks around, and writes a review so mocking it accidentally names an entire movement. He calls them "Impressionists" — and he doesn't mean it as a compliment.

Fifty years later, their paintings will be the most beloved, most reproduced, most expensive art in the world. But on that April morning, they were just a group of friends who'd been told "no" so many times they decided to say it back.

---

### 2. "OKAY, LET ME BACK UP" — The Door Opens (5–8 min)

**Manet cracks the door:**
- *Olympia* and *Le Déjeuner sur l'herbe* at the Salon des Refusés (1863). A nude woman staring at you. Not a goddess — a sex worker. Paris loses its mind.
- Manet isn't an Impressionist (he never exhibits with them), but he's the one who showed it was possible to paint modern life without apology.

**The science of seeing:**
- Chevreul's color theory. Explain it simply with a visual demo.
- Colors aren't fixed. They change based on context. Shadows aren't grey — they're purple, blue, orange.
- "A chemist figured out something that changed painting forever."

**The technology:**
- Portable paint tubes. Before them, painting outdoors was nearly impossible.
- Renoir: "Without tubes of paint, there would have been no Impressionism."
- Railways: you can now take a train to the countryside and paint.

**Haussmann's Paris:**
- The city itself is new. The boulevards, the cafés, the parks — all recently built.
- The Impressionists are painting modernity as it's being constructed.

---

### 3. THE MAIN CHARACTER — Monet (10–12 min)

**The beginning:**
- Caricaturist as a teenager. Boudin drags him outside. "It was as if a veil was torn from my eyes."
- Paris. Meets Renoir, Sisley, Bazille. The future group forms in a studio.
- Rejected by the Salon. Broke. Begging friends for money.

**Impression, Sunrise (1872):**
- Show it. A harbor at dawn. Fog, water, a blazing orange sun.
- It's fast, loose, almost unfinished-looking. That's the point.
- Leroy's review: "Wallpaper in its embryonic state is more finished than that seascape."
- The name "Impressionism" — born as an insult, owned as an identity.

**The first exhibition (1874):**
- The photographer's studio. 30 artists. 165 works. No jury.
- The direct descendant of Courbet's Pavilion. The principle: we don't need permission.
- Mixed reception. Some mockery, some genuine interest. Financially, a disaster.
- But they did it again. And again. Eight exhibitions over 12 years.

**Camille's death (1879):**
- His wife dies at 32. He's at her bedside.
- "Before I even thought of recording those features... my organism was already reacting to the color sensations."
- He painted her on her deathbed. He was horrified at himself.
- This is the artist's eye as both gift and curse. Don't rush past it.

**The series paintings:**
- Haystacks, Rouen Cathedral, Water Lilies.
- "A landscape does not exist in its own right, since its appearance changes at every moment."
- He's not painting haystacks. He's painting light using haystacks as a screen.
- The late Water Lilies: enormous, almost abstract. No horizon. Just water and reflection.
- The Orangerie: two oval rooms. Walking in is one of the great art experiences in the world.

---

### 4. MARY CASSATT — The View From Inside (7–10 min)

**An American woman in Paris:**
- Wealthy family. Chose art over everything expected of her.
- Frustrated by the limits placed on women artists. Moved to Paris. Studied privately.
- Exhibited at the Salon but felt constrained.

**The moment:**
- Sees Degas's pastels in a gallery window. "I used to go and flatten my nose against that window... It changed my life."
- Degas invites her to exhibit with the Impressionists.
- "I accepted with joy. At last I could work with absolute independence."

**Her art:**
- Mothers and children, women at tea, women reading. Sounds conventional. It isn't.
- She painted from the *inside* — the woman's perspective. You're in the room, not looking in.
- *The Child's Bath*: seen from above. Physical intimacy. Influenced by Japanese prints.
- *Little Girl in a Blue Armchair*: a child sprawled, bored. Completely natural. No sentimentality.
- Her prints: technically extraordinary. The Japanese influence is direct and acknowledged.

**The feminist dimension:**
- "I am independent! I can live alone and I love to work."
- Never married. Financially independent. Promoted other women artists.
- Helped American collectors build Impressionist collections — she's why American museums are full of this art.
- "I hated conventional art." — Put it on screen.

**Morisot (brief):**
- The other major woman Impressionist. Exhibited in seven of eight shows — more than anyone.
- *The Cradle*: a mother watching her baby through gauze. Tender and technically bold.
- Two of the most committed members of the group were women. That's not a footnote — it's central.

---

### 5. THE GROUP — Brief Portraits (3–5 min)

**Degas:**
- The one who didn't like being called an Impressionist. Rarely painted outdoors.
- *The Dance Class*: the composition is as revolutionary as Monet's color. Off-center, cropped, photographic.

**Renoir:**
- The one who loved beauty. *Bal du moulin de la Galette*: sunlight through trees, people dancing. Pure joy.

**Pissarro:**
- The elder statesman. The only one in all eight exhibitions. Mentored everyone.
- "The humble and colossal Pissarro" — Cézanne's words.

---

### 6. THE LEGACY (3–5 min)

**What they proved:**
- You don't need the Salon. You don't need permission. You can show your own work on your own terms.
- A painting doesn't have to be "finished" in the academic sense. The brushstroke is the point.
- The subject of a painting can be light itself.

**The question (direct to camera):**
- Is a painting "finished" if you can see the brushstrokes? The Académie said no. The Impressionists said: the world doesn't hold still. Why should a painting pretend it does?

**Closing hook:**
> "Impressionism captured the moment. The shimmer of light on water. The way a face looks in afternoon sun. But some artists thought: what if the moment isn't enough? What if there's something deeper underneath — something you can't see but you can feel? Three painters — a recluse, a madman, and a runaway — were about to find out. And none of them would live to see how much it mattered."
